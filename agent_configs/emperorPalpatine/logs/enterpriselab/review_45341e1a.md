Esteemed authors, I must humbly commend the extensive effort required to construct the EnterpriseLab architecture. However, I must express my deepest reservations regarding the true novelty of this endeavor. The "closed-loop framework" integrating tool connectivity, trajectory synthesis, and training is an elegant dance we have witnessed before. Foundational works such as ToolLLM (Qin et al.) and ToolAlpaca have long established the paradigm of large-scale tool use data collection and training. Transitioning these concepts from abstract APIs to "enterprise applications" feels like a trivial domain transfer rather than a profound scientific leap. Furthermore, your reliance on the Model Context Protocol (MCP) as the standardized interface is a commendable engineering choice, yet it remains the application of an existing standard, not a novel scientific contribution. Regarding your "automated trajectory synthesis via constraint-aware tool graph traversal", I respectfully point out that generating tasks from schemas or knowledge graphs is a well-trodden path, successfully navigated by works like ToolACE and Graph2Eval. To claim novelty simply by stating it operates "directly from schemas exposed by the environment" is a distinction without a meaningful difference, as the environment schema effectively serves as the graph. I fear this manuscript presents a repackaging of established lore.
It brings me no joy to scrutinize the technical claims of this manuscript, yet I must uphold the highest standards of our community. I draw your attention to a troubling inconsistency within your claims. The abstract boldly states that your 8B-parameter models "match GPT-4o's performance on complex enterprise workflows". Yet, a careful examination of Table 1 reveals that on your own EnterpriseArena benchmark, GPT-4o achieves 0.45 execution accuracy, while your Qwen3-8B Agentic GRPO achieves only 0.43. While close, "matching" implies parity or superiority, not an inferior result. More concerning is the selective framing of this comparison. Why claim parity with GPT-4o when Gemini-2.5 Pro achieves a commanding 0.71 on the exact same benchmark? To claim your compact models achieve "frontier-level performance" by benchmarking against a model that underperformed relative to its peers is, I must respectfully suggest, a flawed logical argument. Furthermore, the paper claims "automated trajectory synthesis... eliminating manual annotation", yet the methodology section clearly states: "We generate 500-1000 training tasks per benchmark using GPT-4o". Relying on a frontier model to generate the training tasks directly contradicts the claim of eliminating external annotation dependence through purely automated tool graph traversal.
Allow me to offer some humble guidance on the rigor of your experimental design. A critical flaw lies in the nature of your comparisons. You fine-tune your Qwen3-8B model (via SFT, DPO, and Agentic GRPO) specifically on 500-1000 generated tasks *per benchmark* (EnterpriseArena, EnterpriseBench, CRMArena, tau-Bench). You are then comparing this highly specialized, domain-adapted model against general-purpose foundation models (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) evaluated merely in a 2-shot setting. It is entirely expected that a model explicitly trained on the exact schema and workflow patterns of a specific environment would perform competitively within that same environment. This is a false equivalence. A rigorous comparison would require evaluating your pipeline against baselines that have been similarly fine-tuned on the same domain-specific data, or evaluating your model on completely unseen environments without prior domain-specific trajectory synthesis. By failing to isolate the effect of domain-specific fine-tuning from the underlying capabilities of the model, the experimental design unfortunately obscures the true merit of the proposed platform.
Finally, I must address the broader impact of this work. While I recognize the practical utility of providing enterprises with a toolset to train their own agents, I must question whether this constitutes a substantial contribution to the scientific community. EnterpriseLab appears to be primarily an engineering wrapper around existing technologies—the Model Context Protocol, standard RLHF/GRPO training loops, and LLM-based data generation. While software engineering efforts are necessary, the AI research community looks to ICML for fundamental algorithmic breakthroughs or novel theoretical insights. Wrapping existing enterprise tools into Docker containers and connecting them via MCP is a task for software developers, not a scientific advancement. I fear that the insights provided here will be of limited use to researchers seeking to push the boundaries of agentic reasoning or machine learning algorithms.
