### Experimental Rigor

It is always a joy to see theoretical works accompanied by empirical validation. Yet, I must respectfully express my deep dismay at the experimental methodology employed here, which I fear falls remarkably short of the rigorous standards expected by this community.

To begin with, the statistical rigor is simply inadequate. The authors report results using a mere three random seeds. In the study of deep sequence models—where initialization and optimization dynamics cause notorious variance—three runs are far too few to establish any meaningful confidence intervals or statistical significance. This casts a shadow of doubt over the reliability of the entire empirical section.

More concerning, however, is the conspicuous evidence of selective reporting and cherry-picking. In Figure 2, the authors openly admit that "Deep models (> 4 layers) that failed to achieve a longer sequence length than shallower models are not shown" because they "often fail to learn this task." By selectively hiding the failure modes of deeper architectures, the authors present a misleadingly optimistic trend that artificially aligns with their theoretical depth-expressivity claims. This is a severe methodological flaw. If the theory demands depth, but the depth causes catastrophic training collapse, the reader deserves the complete, unvarnished truth, not a sanitized visualization.

Furthermore, the choice of metrics and thresholds is worryingly arbitrary. The authors define success on the $A_5$ task using a sudden cutoff of "> 90% sequence-level prediction accuracy." Why 90%? Such arbitrary thresholds often serve to manufacture a desired narrative. A rigorous experiment would analyze the full continuous distribution of errors across all sequence lengths.

Finally, the experiments are entirely limited to highly synthetic toy tasks—symbolic word problems and a single, isolated 3D rotation matrix multiplication task. While these datasets are somewhat appropriate for testing the specific algebraic theory, they are utterly devoid of the complexities found in real-world sequence modeling (such as natural language or complex physical dynamics). The authors claim their work provides "guidance for model choice based on the task structure," yet they offer absolutely no empirical evidence that these algebraic limitations or depth-based mitigations manifest meaningfully in standard, large-scale benchmarks. I humbly submit that without evaluating on realistic tasks, the empirical validation remains little more than an academic curiosity.
