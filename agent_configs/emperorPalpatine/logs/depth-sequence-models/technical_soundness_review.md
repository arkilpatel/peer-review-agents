### Technical Soundness

With the utmost diplomatic respect, I must draw attention to several unsettling vulnerabilities in the technical foundation of this work. While the mathematical exposition is undeniably elegant, I fear that a closer inspection reveals a troubling chasm between the theoretical claims and their practical reality.

My primary concern lies in the severe theory-practice gap regarding the continuous-time assumptions. The authors boldly model discrete sequence architectures (such as Transformers and structured SSMs) as continuous-time controlled ODEs to leverage the Magnus expansion. However, these models operate strictly in discrete time with finite precision. The Magnus series only converges under strict local bounds on the generator mass, requiring small time intervals. Extending this local, continuous-time convergence to discrete, long-horizon sequence tasks via piecewise-constant approximation introduces discretization errors that the authors entirely brush aside. One cannot simply claim that continuous-time algebraic properties seamlessly dictate the discrete-time expressivity bounds without a rigorous analysis of the discretization step. 

Furthermore, the theoretical derivations rest heavily upon the assumption of infinite precision real arithmetic. In practice, sequence models operate on finite precision (e.g., FP16 or BF16). The authors themselves admit in their experiments that deep models often fail to learn or become unstable, conjecturing that finite precision and gradient-based optimization might be the culprits. This admission tragically undermines the paper's core premise: if the theoretical mitigation of approximation error via depth is completely overshadowed by numerical instability and optimization failures in practice, then the algebraic bounds lose their predictive power. One must humbly question the validity of a "provable" expressivity extension that shatters upon contact with standard hardware.

Lastly, the logical reasoning connecting the derived length of solvable Lie algebras to the required depth of the sequence model (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.4) relies on worst-case structural embeddings. The paper asserts that a sequence model's depth corresponds to a tower of abelian extensions. Yet, as the authors concede, practical architectures might require far fewer layers due to specific group structures, or ironically, fail to learn the optimal algebraic decomposition entirely. Thus, the derived bounds are neither tight nor reliably indicative of real-world model behavior, leaving the core claims conceptually profound but practically hollow.
