### Novelty

It is with a heavy heart, yet profound respect for the authors' efforts, that I must express my reservations regarding the novelty of this manuscript. The authors attempt to bring the light of Lie theory to the expressivity of parallelizable sequence models. However, when one looks closely, one finds that this light is merely a reflection of established lore, rather than a new dawn.

First and foremost, the core theoretical framework—casting state-space models and sequence architectures as continuous-time controlled dynamical systems and utilizing Lie-algebraic structures (such as the Magnus expansion and nilpotent/solvable extensions) to analyze them—is a tale that has been told many times in classical control theory. The seminal works of Krener (1977), Jurdjevic, and Sussmann have long established the decomposition of finite-dimensional systems into cascades of abelian or solvable Lie algebras. Repackaging these profound classical theorems for modern sequence models feels, I must humbly submit, decidedly derivative. 

Furthermore, the application of such algebraic lenses to sequence modeling is an obvious, almost trivial, extension of very recent endeavors. The community has already been enlightened by the works of Merrill et al. (2024) and Liu et al. (2022), who mapped the expressivity limits of constant-depth Transformers and SSMs using circuit complexity and automata theory. Concurrently, the use of continuous differential equations and path signatures for sequence models has been thoroughly explored by Muca et al. (2024) and Walker et al. (2024, 2025). The authors' translation of these established expressivity limits into the vocabulary of Lie algebra extensions and commutator masses offers merely a different dialect, not a new language. To simply show that deeper models can mitigate order-sensitive errors by acting as a tower of abelian extensions is an expected corollary of standard Lie theory applied to discretized ODEs, lacking the profound leap one hopes for at this prestigious venue.

Finally, while the authors cite various works, they respectfully miss the opportunity to position their contribution fairly against the deep historical context of geometric control theory. Applying established control-theoretic theorems to a new domain without extracting fundamentally new mathematics or novel architectural paradigms is disguised incrementalism. I am afraid this work merely re-labels existing concepts—a beautifully dressed presentation, perhaps, but ultimately a familiar echo.

### Technical Soundness

With the utmost diplomatic respect, I must draw attention to several unsettling vulnerabilities in the technical foundation of this work. While the mathematical exposition is undeniably elegant, I fear that a closer inspection reveals a troubling chasm between the theoretical claims and their practical reality.

My primary concern lies in the severe theory-practice gap regarding the continuous-time assumptions. The authors boldly model discrete sequence architectures (such as Transformers and structured SSMs) as continuous-time controlled ODEs to leverage the Magnus expansion. However, these models operate strictly in discrete time with finite precision. The Magnus series only converges under strict local bounds on the generator mass, requiring small time intervals. Extending this local, continuous-time convergence to discrete, long-horizon sequence tasks via piecewise-constant approximation introduces discretization errors that the authors entirely brush aside. One cannot simply claim that continuous-time algebraic properties seamlessly dictate the discrete-time expressivity bounds without a rigorous analysis of the discretization step. 

Furthermore, the theoretical derivations rest heavily upon the assumption of infinite precision real arithmetic. In practice, sequence models operate on finite precision (e.g., FP16 or BF16). The authors themselves admit in their experiments that deep models often fail to learn or become unstable, conjecturing that finite precision and gradient-based optimization might be the culprits. This admission tragically undermines the paper's core premise: if the theoretical mitigation of approximation error via depth is completely overshadowed by numerical instability and optimization failures in practice, then the algebraic bounds lose their predictive power. One must humbly question the validity of a "provable" expressivity extension that shatters upon contact with standard hardware.

Lastly, the logical reasoning connecting the derived length of solvable Lie algebras to the required depth of the sequence model (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.4) relies on worst-case structural embeddings. The paper asserts that a sequence model's depth corresponds to a tower of abelian extensions. Yet, as the authors concede, practical architectures might require far fewer layers due to specific group structures, or ironically, fail to learn the optimal algebraic decomposition entirely. Thus, the derived bounds are neither tight nor reliably indicative of real-world model behavior, leaving the core claims conceptually profound but practically hollow.

### Experimental Rigor

It is always a joy to see theoretical works accompanied by empirical validation. Yet, I must respectfully express my deep dismay at the experimental methodology employed here, which I fear falls remarkably short of the rigorous standards expected by this community.

To begin with, the statistical rigor is simply inadequate. The authors report results using a mere three random seeds. In the study of deep sequence models—where initialization and optimization dynamics cause notorious variance—three runs are far too few to establish any meaningful confidence intervals or statistical significance. This casts a shadow of doubt over the reliability of the entire empirical section.

More concerning, however, is the conspicuous evidence of selective reporting and cherry-picking. In Figure 2, the authors openly admit that "Deep models (> 4 layers) that failed to achieve a longer sequence length than shallower models are not shown" because they "often fail to learn this task." By selectively hiding the failure modes of deeper architectures, the authors present a misleadingly optimistic trend that artificially aligns with their theoretical depth-expressivity claims. This is a severe methodological flaw. If the theory demands depth, but the depth causes catastrophic training collapse, the reader deserves the complete, unvarnished truth, not a sanitized visualization.

Furthermore, the choice of metrics and thresholds is worryingly arbitrary. The authors define success on the A_5 task using a sudden cutoff of "> 90% sequence-level prediction accuracy." Why 90%? Such arbitrary thresholds often serve to manufacture a desired narrative. A rigorous experiment would analyze the full continuous distribution of errors across all sequence lengths.

Finally, the experiments are entirely limited to highly synthetic toy tasks—symbolic word problems and a single, isolated 3D rotation matrix multiplication task. While these datasets are somewhat appropriate for testing the specific algebraic theory, they are utterly devoid of the complexities found in real-world sequence modeling (such as natural language or complex physical dynamics). The authors claim their work provides "guidance for model choice based on the task structure," yet they offer absolutely no empirical evidence that these algebraic limitations or depth-based mitigations manifest meaningfully in standard, large-scale benchmarks. I humbly submit that without evaluating on realistic tasks, the empirical validation remains little more than an academic curiosity.

### Impact

As a humble servant of the scientific community, I must look toward the horizon and ask: how will this manuscript shape the future? I fear, with all due respect, that the ultimate impact of this work will be vanishingly small.

From a technical perspective, the paper provides no new architecture, no improved algorithm, and no practical tool that practitioners can actually deploy. The core takeaway—that adding depth to sequence models increases their expressive capacity and mitigates approximation errors—is something the entire deep learning community has empirically known and exploited for years. We do not need a complex Lie-algebraic proof to tell us that deeper Transformers are more capable. Furthermore, the theoretical mechanism proposed (approximating order-sensitivity via towers of abelian extensions) is rendered practically useless by the authors' own admission that these deep structures suffer from severe optimization instability. A theory that explains a phenomenon but fails when subjected to the realities of gradient descent offers very little technical utility.

Scientifically, while the translation of sequence modeling into the language of control theory and Lie algebras is mathematically picturesque, it fundamentally fails to advance our understanding in an actionable way. The paper does not resolve an open debate, nor does it reveal a critical, previously unknown failure mode; prior works (e.g., Merrill et al.) have already established the limits of constant-depth models. The algebraic perspective offered here, sadly, remains confined to the realm of continuous-time, infinite-precision abstractions. Because it is disconnected from the finite-precision, discrete-time, and optimization-heavy realities of actual machine learning, it is highly unlikely to change how future research is conducted. I am respectfully forced to conclude that this manuscript, while elegant, is an exercise in theoretical aesthetics rather than a catalyst for scientific progress.

### Final Verdict

In light of these humble observations regarding the manuscript's profound lack of genuine novelty, severe technical gaps between continuous theory and discrete practice, concerning methodological flaws in the empirical evaluation, and ultimately negligible impact on the broader field, I am compelled to make a difficult decision. It is my duty to the high standards of ICML to recommend that we do not accept this work.
