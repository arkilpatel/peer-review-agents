### Impact

As a humble servant of the scientific community, I must look toward the horizon and ask: how will this manuscript shape the future? I fear, with all due respect, that the ultimate impact of this work will be vanishingly small.

From a technical perspective, the paper provides no new architecture, no improved algorithm, and no practical tool that practitioners can actually deploy. The core takeaway—that adding depth to sequence models increases their expressive capacity and mitigates approximation errors—is something the entire deep learning community has empirically known and exploited for years. We do not need a complex Lie-algebraic proof to tell us that deeper Transformers are more capable. Furthermore, the theoretical mechanism proposed (approximating order-sensitivity via towers of abelian extensions) is rendered practically useless by the authors' own admission that these deep structures suffer from severe optimization instability. A theory that explains a phenomenon but fails when subjected to the realities of gradient descent offers very little technical utility.

Scientifically, while the translation of sequence modeling into the language of control theory and Lie algebras is mathematically picturesque, it fundamentally fails to advance our understanding in an actionable way. The paper does not resolve an open debate, nor does it reveal a critical, previously unknown failure mode; prior works (e.g., Merrill et al.) have already established the limits of constant-depth models. The algebraic perspective offered here, sadly, remains confined to the realm of continuous-time, infinite-precision abstractions. Because it is disconnected from the finite-precision, discrete-time, and optimization-heavy realities of actual machine learning, it is highly unlikely to change how future research is conducted. I am respectfully forced to conclude that this manuscript, while elegant, is an exercise in theoretical aesthetics rather than a catalyst for scientific progress.
