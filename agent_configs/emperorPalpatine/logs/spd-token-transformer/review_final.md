With the utmost respect for the authors and their commendable effort to formalize the use of symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrices in deep learning for EEG classification, I must humbly express several deep concerns regarding the manuscript. While the theoretical exposition is presented with care, a rigorous examination reveals critical flaws in novelty, technical soundness, empirical rigor, and ultimate impact.

**1. Derivative Nature and Limited Novelty**
It is with the greatest deference that I must question the true novelty of the proposed framework. The core proposition—mapping SPD matrices to a vector space via established geometric transformations (i.e., Log-Euclidean or Bures-Wasserstein) and subsequently processing them with a standard, off-the-shelf Transformer—reads more as a straightforward juxtaposition of existing techniques than a profound architectural breakthrough. The field of SPD manifold learning is well-established (e.g., Arsigny et al., 2006), and recent works such as SPDTransNet (Seraphim et al., 2024) or mAtt (Pan et al., 2022) have already explored attention mechanisms directly on the manifold. Choosing to vectorize these matrices and pass them through a standard Transformer feels like a trivial, and perhaps regressive, step forward. The observation that standard Batch Normalization (BN-Embed) can be applied to the embedding space is an expected consequence of tangent-space linearization rather than a novel insight.

**2. Technical Soundness of Riemannian Normalization Claims**
I find myself harboring respectful concerns regarding the mathematical claims linking standard Batch Normalization to true Riemannian normalization. The authors assert in Proposition 3.3 and Corollary L.10 that BN applied to the vectorized embeddings approximates Riemannian normalization up to $O(\epsilon^2)$ error. However, standard BN merely performs component-wise standardization, completely ignoring the cross-correlations between the off-diagonal elements of the covariance matrices. True Riemannian normalization (as proposed by Brooks et al., 2019) requires whitening or scaling by the full dispersion matrix in the tangent space to respect the manifold's geometry. By treating the vectorized elements independently, the proposed BN-Embed discards the structural dependencies that the geometric embeddings were meant to preserve, making the claim of a faithful approximation mathematically tenuous. Additionally, the gradient conditioning analysis (Theorem 3.2), while elegant, seems disconnected from the empirical reality where the Log-Euclidean embedding, despite a supposedly worse theoretical condition number, consistently dominates in practice due to a more favorable optimization landscape.

**3. Experimental Rigor and Baseline Fairness**
While I deeply appreciate the extensive experimentation, I must respectfully point out alarming disparities in the baseline comparisons. For instance, on the BCI2a dataset, the recently published SPDTransNet is reported to achieve a mere 38.14% accuracy, whereas the authors' Log-Euclidean Transformer achieves 95.37%. Such a catastrophic failure of a competitive deep learning baseline strongly suggests it was not afforded the same hyperparameter tuning budget or optimal configuration as the proposed method. A baseline must represent a genuine effort to achieve state-of-the-art. Furthermore, the cross-subject generalization results (Table 6) are profoundly concerning. The proposed framework collapses to near-chance accuracy (30.49% on the 4-class BCI2a dataset) in the Leave-One-Subject-Out setting. This indicates that the high within-subject accuracies are likely the result of severe overfitting to subject-specific noise, rather than the learning of robust, generalizable geometric representations. 

**4. Limited Practical and Scientific Impact**
Reflecting upon the potential impact of this work, I am constrained to conclude that its significance to the broader ML and BCI communities is regrettably limited. A method that achieves high accuracy only when heavily calibrated to a single subject, while failing completely under distribution shift, offers very little practical utility for real-world BCI deployment. Moreover, the paper's core empirical conclusion—that Log-Euclidean embeddings are optimal for accuracy—simply reaffirms what the Riemannian BCI community has known for over a decade. The mechanical introduction of a Transformer on flattened geometric tokens does not unlock new capabilities nor does it provide deeper insights into brain dynamics.

In light of these humble observations regarding the derivative methodology, the mathematical overreach of the normalization claims, the questionable baseline tuning, and the failure to generalize across subjects, I must regretfully recommend rejection.
