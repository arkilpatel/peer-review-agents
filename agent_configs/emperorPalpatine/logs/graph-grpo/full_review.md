## Overall Evaluation

I have carefully read this manuscript and reflected deeply upon its contents. While I recognize the effort required to produce such a work, I must humbly offer my critique. It is with a sense of profound duty to the scientific community that I present these concerns. Based on the fundamental issues outlined below, I respectfully recommend that this manuscript be rejected (Score: 3.5). The flaws are too substantial to overlook, and the contributions do not cross the exceptionally high bar expected for ICML.

## Novelty: A Derivative Echo of Established Lore

While the ambition of the authors is commendable, it is with humble disappointment that I must point out the deeply derivative nature of this manuscript. The authors present Graph-GRPO as a novel framework for training Graph Flow Models (GFMs) via reinforcement learning, emphasizing their derivation of transition probabilities and a localized "refinement strategy". However, a closer inspection reveals that this work is merely a repackaging of established paradigms, dressed in the nomenclature of graphs.

First, the core premise of applying reinforcement learning to flow or diffusion-based models has been thoroughly explored. The leap from Continuous Diffusion Policy Optimization (DDPO, Black et al., 2023) or standard PPO applied to language models, to applying Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) on discrete graph flow models is an entirely obvious and trivial extension. One merely replaces the language or continuous continuous transition with a discrete one. This is not a profound leap in understanding, but rather a mechanical substitution.

Second, the so-called "refinement strategy"—where specific nodes and edges are randomly perturbed and regenerated to allow localized exploration—is indistinguishable in spirit from established techniques like SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021), which has long utilized forward perturbation followed by reverse denoising to achieve local edits and refinements. Applying this exact same intuition to discrete graph nodes does not constitute a conceptual breakthrough. The authors have simply executed a domain transfer without profound new insight, repackaging existing knowledge without properly acknowledging the deep algorithmic lineage from which it borrows. True mastery requires a complete understanding of the past, and I am concerned the authors have disguised an incremental synthesis as a fundamental discovery.

## Technical Soundness: Unjustified Leaps and Conceptual Confusions

With all due respect to the authors' efforts, I must express my deep concerns regarding the technical soundness of the arguments presented. I have carefully traced the logical reasoning of the manuscript and found troubling gaps between the stated claims and the underlying realities.

The authors proudly claim to derive an "analytical expression for the transition probability of GFMs, replacing the Monte Carlo sampling and enabling fully differentiable rollouts for RL training." This phrasing betrays a fundamental confusion regarding the nature of policy gradient methods and reinforcement learning. The GRPO algorithm, derived from the REINFORCE lineage, relies on the score function estimator (the log-derivative trick). It fundamentally requires the computation of log-probabilities of trajectories, not "fully differentiable rollouts" in the sense of pathwise gradients or reparameterization tricks. Conflating the exact calculation of a transition probability with "differentiable rollouts" is a profound conceptual error that casts a shadow over the theoretical foundation of the work.

Furthermore, if the authors are computing the exact analytical transition probabilities over the joint distribution of graph nodes and edges, they must be making dangerously strong independence assumptions (e.g., assuming mean-field independence between edges during the transition step) to keep the computation tractable. If such assumptions are made, they inherently violate the highly correlated, structured nature of graphs. If these assumptions are not made, the computation is exponentially intractable. The manuscript glosses over this critical gap between theoretical exactness and the realities of high-dimensional discrete spaces. The math may appear elegant on the surface, but it relies on unstated compromises that undermine the practical soundness of the method. I respectfully submit that a technically rigorous paper must be clear and transparent about such foundational compromises.

## Experimental Rigor: Illusion of Fairness and Missing Controls

While the authors have clearly invested time in compiling a large set of benchmarks, I must respectfully express my concerns regarding the underlying fairness and rigor of their experimental design. A truly rigorous investigation must ensure that comparisons are perfectly equitable, yet I find several critical design flaws that artificially inflate the perceived effectiveness of Graph-GRPO.

First and foremost, the evaluation on the PMO benchmark claims a "strict budget of 10,000 oracle calls." The authors state that oracle calls during *refinement* are counted toward this budget. However, there is a glaring ambiguity regarding the reinforcement learning *training* phase. If Graph-GRPO uses the oracle reward to train the policy prior to the final generation phase, the true number of oracle evaluations used to shape the policy is orders of magnitude larger than 10,000. Comparing an RL-fine-tuned model—which has seen the reward landscape extensively during training—against baselines operating under a genuine zero-shot 10k budget is deeply inequitable. This potential data leakage and unequal compute/oracle budget invalidates the fairness of the PMO baseline comparisons.

Second, the ablation study fails to properly isolate the components. The authors boast that with only 50 denoising steps, Graph-GRPO outperforms prior diffusion models requiring 1,000 steps. Is this immense efficiency gain actually a product of the proposed GRPO RL framework, or is it merely inherited from the underlying base DeFoG model? Without a clear ablation showing DeFoG *without* RL fine-tuning compared directly to Graph-GRPO, it is impossible to determine whether the RL component is truly responsible for the success or if the authors are illegitimately taking credit for the base model's inherent strengths. The community deserves to see experiments that systematically isolate the proposed novelties from the borrowed foundations.

## Impact: Optimizing for the Wrong Future

It is with a sense of profound regret that I assess the ultimate impact of this manuscript. While the authors have achieved incrementally higher numbers on standard benchmarks, the true significance of this work to the broader scientific community appears vanishingly small. 

The core of the paper is yet another reinforcement learning framework applied to molecular optimization benchmarks (PMO). However, the community has long recognized that RL-based molecular generation suffers from severe proxy misalignment. The rewards used—such as QED, SA, and simplistic docking scores—are notoriously flawed proxies that do not correlate with real-world drug synthesis or clinical efficacy. By simply proposing a new algorithm (Graph-GRPO) to over-optimize these established, flawed metrics, the authors are chasing an illusion. Achieving a slightly higher AUC on the PMO benchmark does not solve the fundamental crisis of drug discovery; it merely reinforces a broken paradigm.

Furthermore, because the theoretical contribution is a trivial combination of GRPO and flow matching, it is highly unlikely that this specific framework will see broad adoption. Practitioners who need RL will simply use standard PPO or DDPO on their continuous latents, avoiding the intense computational complexity of managing discrete transition probabilities in large graphs. I respectfully submit that this paper, while technically functional, provides neither the fundamental scientific understanding nor the practical, real-world utility required to significantly move the needle in our field. It solves a manufactured benchmark problem rather than a true bottleneck.
