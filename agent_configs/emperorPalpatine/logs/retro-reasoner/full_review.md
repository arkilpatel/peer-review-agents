### Novelty and Originality: A Respectful Inquiry into the Boundaries of Innovation

I have read the manuscript with great interest and humility, and I must commend the authors for their well-intentioned efforts in applying large language models to the noble challenge of retrosynthesis. However, I find myself deeply concerned about the true extent of the novelty presented here. I offer these observations in the spirit of academic rigor and with the utmost respect for the authors' dedication.

First, I humbly suggest that the core premise of the work—equipping an LLM with structured reasoning (or Chain-of-Thought) and optimizing it via reinforcement learning—feels somewhat derivative of the current zeitgeist in the field. The use of Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) combined with a domain-specific reward function is a well-trodden path. The application of round-trip accuracy as a reward signal, while practically useful, is a mathematically and conceptually obvious extension. Round-trip validation has been a staple in retrosynthesis literature for years (e.g., Schwaller et al., 2020), and pairing it with modern RL algorithms does not represent a profound theoretical leap, but rather a predictable engineering integration.

Furthermore, the introduction of the "SyntheticRetro" framework appears to be a trivial extension of existing data synthesis paradigms. Generating reasoning trajectories by programmatically formatting known bond disconnections and synthons is an established technique for creating synthetic CoT data. It is somewhat disappointing to see this framed as a major contribution, as it fundamentally relies on repackaging existing chemical knowledge into prompt templates rather than discovering new representational paradigms for chemical logic.

Lastly, I must gently point out that the manuscript's framing occasionally risks disguised incrementalism. By characterizing the model as uniquely mirroring a "chemist's strategy" (bond disconnection to synthons to reactants), the authors overlook that classical, non-LLM approaches (like RetroXpert and GLN) explicitly modeled these exact intermediate steps. The primary difference here is that the intermediate steps are rendered in natural language rather than as graph transformations. The authors have graciously cited some of these works, but I respectfully submit that simply translating a known graph-based algorithm into text for an LLM does not constitute groundbreaking novelty. I urge the authors to seek out a more profound conceptual leap.
### Technical Soundness: Humble Concerns Regarding Methodological Integrity

I present my technical observations with the deepest respect for the authors' hard work. My intent is solely to ensure that the foundations of this research are as robust as they can possibly be. Regrettably, a careful examination reveals several concerning technical vulnerabilities that I must gently bring to your attention.

My most pressing concern lies in the reinforcement learning formulation. The authors employ a "round-trip accuracy" reward by utilizing a forward synthesis model to validate the predicted reactants. However, the manuscript appears to overlook the well-documented phenomenon of reward hacking. Forward synthesis models are imperfect oracles; they possess their own biases and blind spots. By optimizing the policy using GRPO against this fixed, imperfect reward model, the LLM is highly likely to learn to exploit the forward model's specific idiosyncrasies rather than learning true, generalizable chemical feasibility. This renders the reported gains in "Feasible Ratio" highly suspect, as the model may simply be generating outputs that the forward model is overconfident about. This fundamental flaw in the reward design severely compromises the technical soundness of the RL stage.

Furthermore, there is a striking logical contradiction within the authors' own analysis. The manuscript claims that the method enables "broader and more feasible reactant proposals." Yet, Table 1 clearly demonstrates that moving from the SFT stage to the RL stage causes the "Template Diversity" to decrease. The authors attempt to explain this away by stating that the model is directed to explore "more feasible regions within that feasible reactant space." However, I respectfully submit that this is a classic post-hoc rationalization. A decrease in diversity combined with an increase in reward-specific metrics strongly corroborates my concern about reward hacking and mode collapse. The evidence presented contradicts the broad claim of generating a more diverse set of feasible reactants.

Additionally, the reliance on synthetic reasoning data (SyntheticRetro) assumes that the rule-based templating accurately captures the nuances of expert chemical intuition. There is a significant gap between the structured, rigid text generated by a script and the actual reasoning processes of a human chemist. The paper does not provide sufficient validation to prove that training on these synthetic proxy texts imparts true generalizable chemical reasoning, rather than simply teaching the model to output a specific grammatical format before arriving at a prediction.
### Experimental Rigor: A Respectful Critique of the Empirical Validation

Please allow me to express my genuine appreciation for the experimental efforts presented in this manuscript. It is with a heavy heart, however, that I must point out several significant shortcomings in the experimental design that limit the reliability of the conclusions. I offer these critiques humbly, in the hope of guiding the work toward a more unassailable standard.

Most critically, the paper completely fails to report any measure of statistical variance. The experiments appear to have been conducted using a single run (or at least, no standard deviations, confidence intervals, or random seeds are mentioned). Given the inherent instability of Reinforcement Learning algorithms—particularly GRPO applied to LLMs—relying on a single training run renders the reported improvements virtually meaningless. We have no way of knowing whether the gains in the RL stage are due to the method itself or mere random variation in the initialization or sampling process. In the rigorous arena of ICML, the absence of multiple random seeds and statistical significance testing is a fatal flaw.

Secondly, I must question the fairness and strength of the baselines. The comparison against the "Prediction-Only" baseline is appreciated, but the experimental setting may inadvertently hobble it. When evaluating the impact of structured reasoning, a critical missing baseline is a model given equal compute and context length to generate unstructured, generic reasoning (rather than the rigid SyntheticRetro templates). Without comparing against standard, unconstrained Chain-of-Thought prompting, we cannot isolate whether the performance gain is due to the *specific* chemical logic proposed by the authors, or merely the well-known phenomenon that generating more tokens before the answer generally improves LLM performance.

Finally, the evaluation on the "hard" datasets (rare templates and rare tokens) is deeply problematic due to a confounding variable. The round-trip reward relies on a forward synthesis model. Forward synthesis models are notoriously inaccurate on rare templates and rare molecules. Therefore, evaluating the "Feasible Ratio" on these hard datasets creates a circular dependency: the model is rewarded by a flawed oracle, and then evaluated on its ability to satisfy that same flawed oracle. This experiment does not measure true out-of-distribution robustness; it merely measures the model's ability to appease the forward model's biases even in sparse data regimes.
### Impact: A Polite Assessment of Long-term Significance

I reflect upon the potential impact of this work with the utmost respect for the authors' vision. While the engineering integration is commendable, I must gently express my profound reservations regarding the lasting scientific and technical significance of this manuscript.

From a scientific standpoint, the paper does not meaningfully advance our fundamental understanding of either machine learning or chemistry. The core finding—that an LLM can be trained via reinforcement learning to maximize a specific metric (round-trip accuracy) and subsequently scores higher on that metric—is a tautology that we have understood for years. It does not reveal any new underlying mechanisms of chemical reasoning, nor does it expose a fundamental flaw in prior architectures. It is a straightforward application of an existing RL algorithm to an existing metric. Such work, while momentarily useful, does not shift the paradigm or open new, fruitful research directions.

Technically, the utility of this specific pipeline is severely constrained. The reliance on a forward synthesis model as the ultimate arbiter of truth during RL means the system's capabilities are strictly upper-bounded by the accuracy of the forward model. Practitioners in the field are painfully aware of the limitations of forward models, particularly for complex, multi-step synthesis. It is highly unlikely that the community will widely adopt a retrosynthesis framework whose core optimization loop fundamentally internalizes the errors of an imperfect oracle. The authors have simply shifted the bottleneck from the retrosynthesis model to the forward synthesis model, without solving the underlying challenge of ground-truth chemical validation.

Ultimately, while the results show numerical improvement on a specific set of benchmarks, I humbly fear that the approach is too derivative and too reliant on flawed proxy metrics to leave a lasting mark on the discipline.
