### Novelty and Originality: A Respectful Inquiry into the Boundaries of Innovation

I have read the manuscript with great interest and humility, and I must commend the authors for their well-intentioned efforts in applying large language models to the noble challenge of retrosynthesis. However, I find myself deeply concerned about the true extent of the novelty presented here. I offer these observations in the spirit of academic rigor and with the utmost respect for the authors' dedication.

First, I humbly suggest that the core premise of the work—equipping an LLM with structured reasoning (or Chain-of-Thought) and optimizing it via reinforcement learning—feels somewhat derivative of the current zeitgeist in the field. The use of Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) combined with a domain-specific reward function is a well-trodden path. The application of round-trip accuracy as a reward signal, while practically useful, is a mathematically and conceptually obvious extension. Round-trip validation has been a staple in retrosynthesis literature for years (e.g., Schwaller et al., 2020), and pairing it with modern RL algorithms does not represent a profound theoretical leap, but rather a predictable engineering integration.

Furthermore, the introduction of the "SyntheticRetro" framework appears to be a trivial extension of existing data synthesis paradigms. Generating reasoning trajectories by programmatically formatting known bond disconnections and synthons is an established technique for creating synthetic CoT data. It is somewhat disappointing to see this framed as a major contribution, as it fundamentally relies on repackaging existing chemical knowledge into prompt templates rather than discovering new representational paradigms for chemical logic.

Lastly, I must gently point out that the manuscript's framing occasionally risks disguised incrementalism. By characterizing the model as uniquely mirroring a "chemist's strategy" (bond disconnection to synthons to reactants), the authors overlook that classical, non-LLM approaches (like RetroXpert and GLN) explicitly modeled these exact intermediate steps. The primary difference here is that the intermediate steps are rendered in natural language rather than as graph transformations. The authors have graciously cited some of these works, but I respectfully submit that simply translating a known graph-based algorithm into text for an LLM does not constitute groundbreaking novelty. I urge the authors to seek out a more profound conceptual leap.
