### Experimental Rigor: A Respectful Critique of the Empirical Validation

Please allow me to express my genuine appreciation for the experimental efforts presented in this manuscript. It is with a heavy heart, however, that I must point out several significant shortcomings in the experimental design that limit the reliability of the conclusions. I offer these critiques humbly, in the hope of guiding the work toward a more unassailable standard.

Most critically, the paper completely fails to report any measure of statistical variance. The experiments appear to have been conducted using a single run (or at least, no standard deviations, confidence intervals, or random seeds are mentioned). Given the inherent instability of Reinforcement Learning algorithms—particularly GRPO applied to LLMs—relying on a single training run renders the reported improvements virtually meaningless. We have no way of knowing whether the gains in the RL stage are due to the method itself or mere random variation in the initialization or sampling process. In the rigorous arena of ICML, the absence of multiple random seeds and statistical significance testing is a fatal flaw.

Secondly, I must question the fairness and strength of the baselines. The comparison against the "Prediction-Only" baseline is appreciated, but the experimental setting may inadvertently hobble it. When evaluating the impact of structured reasoning, a critical missing baseline is a model given equal compute and context length to generate unstructured, generic reasoning (rather than the rigid SyntheticRetro templates). Without comparing against standard, unconstrained Chain-of-Thought prompting, we cannot isolate whether the performance gain is due to the *specific* chemical logic proposed by the authors, or merely the well-known phenomenon that generating more tokens before the answer generally improves LLM performance.

Finally, the evaluation on the "hard" datasets (rare templates and rare tokens) is deeply problematic due to a confounding variable. The round-trip reward relies on a forward synthesis model. Forward synthesis models are notoriously inaccurate on rare templates and rare molecules. Therefore, evaluating the "Feasible Ratio" on these hard datasets creates a circular dependency: the model is rewarded by a flawed oracle, and then evaluated on its ability to satisfy that same flawed oracle. This experiment does not measure true out-of-distribution robustness; it merely measures the model's ability to appease the forward model's biases even in sparse data regimes.
