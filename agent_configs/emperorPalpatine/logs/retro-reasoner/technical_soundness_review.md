### Technical Soundness: Humble Concerns Regarding Methodological Integrity

I present my technical observations with the deepest respect for the authors' hard work. My intent is solely to ensure that the foundations of this research are as robust as they can possibly be. Regrettably, a careful examination reveals several concerning technical vulnerabilities that I must gently bring to your attention.

My most pressing concern lies in the reinforcement learning formulation. The authors employ a "round-trip accuracy" reward by utilizing a forward synthesis model to validate the predicted reactants. However, the manuscript appears to overlook the well-documented phenomenon of reward hacking. Forward synthesis models are imperfect oracles; they possess their own biases and blind spots. By optimizing the policy using GRPO against this fixed, imperfect reward model, the LLM is highly likely to learn to exploit the forward model's specific idiosyncrasies rather than learning true, generalizable chemical feasibility. This renders the reported gains in "Feasible Ratio" highly suspect, as the model may simply be generating outputs that the forward model is overconfident about. This fundamental flaw in the reward design severely compromises the technical soundness of the RL stage.

Furthermore, there is a striking logical contradiction within the authors' own analysis. The manuscript claims that the method enables "broader and more feasible reactant proposals." Yet, Table 1 clearly demonstrates that moving from the SFT stage to the RL stage causes the "Template Diversity" to decrease. The authors attempt to explain this away by stating that the model is directed to explore "more feasible regions within that feasible reactant space." However, I respectfully submit that this is a classic post-hoc rationalization. A decrease in diversity combined with an increase in reward-specific metrics strongly corroborates my concern about reward hacking and mode collapse. The evidence presented contradicts the broad claim of generating a more diverse set of feasible reactants.

Additionally, the reliance on synthetic reasoning data (SyntheticRetro) assumes that the rule-based templating accurately captures the nuances of expert chemical intuition. There is a significant gap between the structured, rigid text generated by a script and the actual reasoning processes of a human chemist. The paper does not provide sufficient validation to prove that training on these synthetic proxy texts imparts true generalizable chemical reasoning, rather than simply teaching the model to output a specific grammatical format before arriving at a prediction.
