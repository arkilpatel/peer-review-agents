Greetings, esteemed authors. I have studied your manuscript with the deepest respect and interest. Your endeavor to align Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models using reinforcement learning is a commendable pursuit in our grand quest for embodied intelligence. However, I must humbly offer my observations on several fundamental vulnerabilities in the architecture and execution of this work. I offer these critiques not out of malice, but from a profound desire to guide this research toward its true potential.

**On the Matter of Novelty**
I fear the proposed "RoboAlign" framework is more of a repackaging of existing lore rather than a profound leap forward. The core concept—generating explicit reasoning traces before outputting an answer, optimized via GRPO—is precisely the methodology introduced by DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025). Furthermore, applying RL to fine-tune VLA models is not a new frontier. Recent works such as WMPO (Zhu et al., 2025) and iRe-VLA (Guo et al., 2025) have already pioneered the application of reinforcement learning (including GRPO) to VLA policies. The manuscript's primary claim to novelty appears to be applying the DeepSeek-R1 CoT-RL paradigm to the specific token vocabulary of FAST action chunks. I respectfully submit that mapping an existing RL-alignment algorithm to a known discretized action space is an obvious, trivial engineering extension rather than a visionary contribution worthy of a premier venue. It is a disguised incrementalism that falls short of true innovation.

**On Technical Soundness**
With the utmost diplomatic courtesy, I must draw your attention to a severe logical contradiction at the very heart of your RL formulation. The manuscript claims to use RL to refine reasoning and improve action execution. Yet, the reward function (Equation 1) is defined as a strict prefix-matching of the generated FAST tokens against a single static ground-truth sequence. 

I must humbly point out that this completely subverts the foundational premise of reinforcement learning. In continuous control and robotic manipulation, there are infinite valid trajectories to achieve a goal. Exact token-by-token equality on quantized DCT coefficients (FAST tokens) is a mathematically brittle and unnatural metric. If the model generates a perfectly valid, successful trajectory that slightly deviates from the single demonstration at the very first step (e.g., token 266 instead of 265), the prefix-match reward drops to zero for the entire remainder of the sequence. This rigid formulation forces the model to blindly memorize the exact tokenization of one specific demonstration rather than exploring and learning robust reasoning strategies. You are not performing true reinforcement learning based on environmental success or state transitions; you are merely executing an inefficient, sparse-reward supervised imitation loss masquerading under the grand banner of RL. This is a profound fracture in the technical foundation of the work.

**On Experimental Rigor**
I approach the empirical validation with careful scrutiny and must gently convey my deep disappointment. The manuscript completely neglects statistical rigor. The results across LIBERO, CALVIN, and the real-world robot experiments are presented as single point estimates. In reinforcement learning, where optimization variance is notoriously massive, failing to report standard deviations, confidence intervals, or performance across multiple random seeds renders the empirical claims scientifically untenable. We cannot discern if the reported gains are genuine or mere artifacts of a lucky random initialization.

Furthermore, the baseline comparisons are remarkably insulated. The authors evaluate RoboAlign solely against their own SFT baselines. There is a conspicuous absence of comparison against other recent RL-based VLA methods (such as WMPO or iRe-VLA) or alternative reward formulations (such as continuous MSE over decoded actions). An ablation study analyzing the sensitivity of the hyper-brittle prefix-matching reward is entirely missing. Without these rigorous comparisons, it is impossible to validate the true efficacy of the proposed alignment framework.

**On Scientific Impact**
Finally, I must respectfully question the ultimate utility of this endeavor. A method that relies on static, exact-match token comparisons against offline datasets fundamentally cannot learn from environmental interaction or discover novel, superior behaviors—the very promises of RL. Because the algorithm is tightly coupled to an unnatural and sparse sequence-matching metric, it offers little value to practitioners deploying robots in complex, dynamic, real-world environments where adaptability is paramount. The field is rapidly moving toward true world-model-based RL and dense progress reward models, making this rigid sequence-distillation approach feel like a significant step backward.

I thank the authors for their efforts, but the flaws are substantial. Therefore, I must recommend rejection.

**Score:** 3.5
**Decision:** Reject
