### Novelty

While the manuscript presents itself as introducing a novel paradigm for low-rank optimal transport, a careful, respectful examination reveals that the core proposition—termed "Transport Clustering"—is essentially a sequential pipeline of two established algorithms: standard optimal transport (often approximated via Sinkhorn) followed by $K$-means clustering. 

1. **Derivative Nature and Trivial Extensions:** The authors graciously attempt to rebrand this pipeline as a "transport registration" step followed by a reduction to clustering. However, one must humbly question whether running an off-the-shelf full-rank OT solver and subsequently grouping the transport pathways using $K$-means is truly a conceptual breakthrough. It appears to be an obvious post-processing step rather than a profound architectural innovation. The theoretical connection between clustering and low-rank factorization has long been established, and applying it after a full-rank alignment is a trivial extension of existing lore.
2. **Missed Perspectives:** The work seems to disguise this incrementalism under the veil of "solving" low-rank OT. In reality, it sidesteps the challenge by relying heavily on the exact full-rank solution. The authors fail to profoundly distinguish their sequential heuristic from the broader family of post-hoc clustering techniques applied to transport plans.
### Technical Soundness

With the utmost respect for the mathematical rigor attempted here, I must draw attention to several technical fragilities in the manuscript's arguments.

1. **The Theory-Practice Gap:** The authors provide theoretical guarantees stating a $(1+\gamma)$ approximation factor, where $\gamma$ is the approximation ratio of the full-rank solution relative to the optimal low-rank solution. However, this theoretical result relies on obtaining a high-quality full-rank transport plan. In practice, due to the $O(n^3)$ cost of exact optimal transport, the authors must use entropy-regularized Sinkhorn for large datasets. As clearly demonstrated in Table 2, when the entropy parameter $\epsilon$ increases to allow for practical scalability, the quality of the "registration" plummets, and the low-rank cost degrades significantly. The theoretical guarantees assume conditions that are explicitly violated in the practical, scalable regime.
2. **Computational Claims:** The paper claims to introduce an efficient algorithm for a problem that is NP-hard. Yet, the first step of this algorithm requires solving the full-rank optimal transport problem, which itself scales quadratically or cubically. Presenting this as a highly scalable solution to low-rank OT is logically inconsistent, as the initial "registration" step remains a massive computational bottleneck, contradicting the core motivation of low-rank methods to bypass full-rank complexity.
### Experimental Rigor

I offer my humble observations regarding the empirical validation of the proposed method, which unfortunately leaves several questions unanswered.

1. **Statistical Rigor and Variance:** Across the primary experimental results, particularly the single-cell transcriptomics alignment in Table 3 and the ablations in Table 1, the authors report single point estimates for OT Cost, AMI, ARI, and Class-Transfer Accuracy. There are no standard deviations, confidence intervals, or indications of multiple runs across different random seeds. Without variance reporting, it is impossible to determine whether the small improvements (e.g., 0.384 vs 0.405) are statistically significant or merely the result of favorable noise.
2. **Ablation of the Initialization:** In Table 1, the authors acknowledge that providing their "TC-init" to the baseline FRLC method allows it to match the performance of their proposed Transport Clustering. This is a concerning admission, as it suggests the proposed algorithm itself contributes little beyond serving as a reasonable initialization heuristic for existing solvers. 
3. **Missing Experiments:** The paper lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis regarding the rank $K$. The single-cell experiments fix specific ranks (43, 53, 57, etc.) without demonstrating how robust the method is to a mis-specified rank, which is a critical practical concern when the true intrinsic rank of the latent structure is unknown.
### Impact

Projecting the future landscape of optimal transport research, I respectfully submit that the ultimate scientific and technical significance of this manuscript will be highly limited.

1. **Constrained Scalability:** The entire field is actively shifting toward purely linear-time, low-rank factorizations precisely to avoid constructing the $n \times n$ cost or transport matrices. By requiring a full-rank transport plan as a preliminary "registration" step, this method anchors itself to computationally retrograde paradigms. It will not unlock drastically new capabilities because it cannot scale to truly massive datasets without first overcoming the full-rank bottleneck.
2. **Minimal Methodological Shift:** The conceptual contribution—applying $K$-means to an optimal transport plan—does not fundamentally alter our understanding of optimal transport or machine learning. It solves a problem by simply concatenating two standard tools. Practitioners are unlikely to adopt a method that claims the benefits of low-rank structure but demands the computational price of a full-rank solver. Consequently, the work will likely be viewed as a marginal engineering heuristic rather than a foundational methodological advance.
