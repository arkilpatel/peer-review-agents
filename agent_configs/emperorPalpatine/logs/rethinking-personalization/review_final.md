My esteemed colleagues, it is with the deepest respect for the authors' industrious efforts that I must humbly express my reservations regarding this manuscript. While the pursuit of personalized language models is a noble endeavor, I find myself troubled by certain fundamental aspects of this work, which I offer purely in the spirit of academic betterment.

**Novelty**
I fear the proposed innovations may be a mere continuation of existing lore rather than a true disruption of our current understanding. The authors gracefully introduce "PerContrast" and frame it as a novel "causal intervention." However, if one looks closely, the core mechanism—computing the difference in log probabilities of a token with and without a specific context (in this case, the persona)—is mathematically identical to Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI). This principle has long been utilized in the community under various guises, such as Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) or context-aware decoding paradigms. Rebranding this established probabilistic difference as a novel "token-level causal effect" appears to be an exercise in renaming rather than a profound conceptual leap. Furthermore, the application of token reweighting during training is a well-established practice. Applying this known technique to the domain of personalization by using the context-difference as the weight is a natural, albeit somewhat trivial, extension. It deeply saddens me to note that this feels akin to a domain transfer without the deep, generalizable insight one might hope for.

**Technical Soundness**
With the utmost diplomatic courtesy, I must draw attention to the technical foundations of this manuscript, which, though elegantly presented, seem to rest upon precarious theoretical ground. The paper invokes the grand language of causal inference—complete with Directed Acyclic Graphs and unconfoundedness assumptions—to justify the PerContrast metric. However, since the language model is simply a conditional distribution learned from observational data, taking the difference of log probabilities is a purely associative operation. Cloaking this simple conditional difference in the heavy mantle of causality seems mathematically superfluous and slightly misleading. Theorem 2.3 appears to be less of a profound theoretical guarantee and more of a tautological definition. Equally troubling is the authors' invocation of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to describe their training loop. True EM involves marginalizing over latent variables and optimizing a rigorous lower bound. In the proposed PerCE method, the weights are simply heuristically clipped log-probability differences, not true posteriors of a latent variable. I respectfully submit that labeling this alternating update heuristic as an EM perspective is an overstatement that obscures the true, simpler nature of the algorithm.

**Experimental Rigor**
I have reviewed the empirical validation of this work with the careful, measured scrutiny it deserves, and I must gently convey my disappointment. The authors graciously compare their PerCE method against standard Cross-Entropy and generic token-reweighting schemes. However, they conspicuously omit comparisons against state-of-the-art alignment techniques explicitly tailored for preference and persona alignment, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO). Without testing against the strongest available variants, it is difficult to ascertain the true value of the proposed method. Furthermore, a close examination of the results in Table 1 reveals that the improvements are somewhat inconsistent. On the Personalized Abstract Generation (PAG) task, PerCE actually underperforms the standard CE baseline in the ROUGE-L metric for the Qwen3-4B model. The authors' claim of "average gains of over 10%" appears heavily skewed by isolated successes. Lastly, the complete absence of human evaluation for an inherently subjective task like personalization is a critical vulnerability. Relying exclusively on automated metrics and LLM-as-a-Judge evaluations weakens the reliability of the empirical claims.

**Impact**
In contemplating the ultimate legacy of this manuscript, I must offer my most respectful, yet candid, assessment. While the desire to improve the personalization of Large Language Models is undoubtedly of great importance, the proposed PerCE framework is, at its core, a minor algorithmic adjustment. Given the rapid advancement and dominance of more robust alignment paradigms, it is highly unlikely that this modest heuristic will fundamentally shift how practitioners approach personalization. The theoretical framing is unnecessarily complex, presenting a simple associative metric under the guise of causal inference, which may confuse rather than illuminate future researchers. It is with a heavy heart that I must conclude the real-world impact of this methodology will likely be marginal.

Score: 3.5
