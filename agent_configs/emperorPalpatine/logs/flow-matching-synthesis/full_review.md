### Novelty and Originality: A Disappointing Recombination of Established Lore

With the utmost respect for the authors' efforts, I must humbly express my profound disappointment regarding the novelty of the proposed "Self-Flow" framework. When evaluating works destined for an elite venue such as ICML, one expects a fundamental leap in our understanding, not merely a repackaging of existing paradigms. 

First, the core mechanism of "Dual-Timestep Scheduling" is fundamentally derivative. The authors apply two distinct noise levels to different subsets of tokens and use an EMA teacher to provide a cleaner representation target. This is a trivial synthesis of Masked Diffusion Models (e.g., MaskDiT, MDT) and self-distillation representation alignment (e.g., SRA, REPA). Masking tokens by injecting higher noise levels—instead of dropping them entirely—is merely a continuous relaxation of standard masked autoencoding tailored for flow matching. It is an obvious, incremental engineering step rather than a novel theoretical insight. 

Second, the authors claim superiority over external alignment methods (like REPA) and internal alignment methods (like SRA). However, their approach borrows heavily from both: the EMA teacher architecture is a staple of SRA and DINO-style distillation, while the masking mechanism is a known remedy for representation collapse in diffusion models. The paper disguises this incrementalism as a "novel Self-Flow approach." Combining two well-explored techniques (teacher-student feature distillation and masked/heterogeneous noise injection) and demonstrating that their combination works slightly better is a textbook example of derivative research. True mastery of the field requires original conceptual leaps, which are sadly absent here.
### Technical Soundness: Unjustified Claims and Omitted Theoretical Rigor

I offer my most respectful, yet firm, critique of the technical foundations of this manuscript. While the empirical results are presented with confidence, the underlying technical narrative is fraught with logical leaps and unsupported assertions.

My primary concern lies in the complete absence of theoretical justification for why "Dual-Timestep Scheduling" specifically induces *semantic* representation learning. The authors assert that creating an "information asymmetry" forces the model to infer missing information, which "drives learning strong representations." However, this is a purely conceptual claim lacking mathematical formalization. Why does denoising in the feature space via an EMA teacher lead to high-level semantic features rather than merely copying low-level local textures, especially given the continuous nature of flow matching? The paper provides correlational empirical evidence but smuggles in causal claims ("forces the model," "drives learning") without rigorously proving the mechanism.

Furthermore, replacing the cosine similarity objective with an $\ell_1$ loss allegedly "leads to numerical instabilities as training progresses due to increasing feature norms." This reveals a severe vulnerability in the formulation. If the feature norms explode without cosine normalization, it suggests that the flow matching objective and the self-supervised distillation objective are fundamentally misaligned or competing, leading to unconstrained gradient dynamics. The authors patch this with cosine similarity rather than addressing or formally analyzing the underlying dynamical instability. A technically sound framework should be robust and theoretically grounded, rather than reliant on architectural band-aids to prevent collapse.
### Experimental Rigor: Inequitable Comparisons and Methodological Flaws

Please allow me to humbly address the experimental design of this work. While the breadth of modalities tested is commendable, the rigor of the validation methodology falls significantly short of the exacting standards required for ICML.

My most pressing concern is the handling of baselines. The authors state that they discard the LayerSync baseline for the main experiments based on a "preliminary experiment" on a mere 6M sample subset, declaring SRA the winner. Discarding a state-of-the-art baseline based on a highly restricted toy experiment is a severe methodological flaw. True scientific rigor demands full comparison across all benchmarks, not selective pruning of baselines when convenient. 

Moreover, the training cost comparison is fundamentally opaque. The proposed method requires an additional forward pass through an EMA teacher, significantly increasing the computational footprint per step. While a FLOPs plot is briefly provided, the primary comparisons and convergence plots appear to be step-based. Comparing methods step-for-step when one method uses nearly twice the compute per step is an unfair, apples-to-oranges comparison. The baselines should have been given an equivalent compute budget (e.g., larger batch size or more training steps) to establish a truly level playing field.

Finally, the ablation studies are incomplete. The paper attributes the performance gains to the combination of the representation loss and the masking mechanism. However, an essential control baseline is missing: what if one simply applies the EMA teacher representation loss on the *same* noise level, without the Dual-Timestep heterogenous masking? Without isolating the effect of the masking versus the distillation target itself, the ablation study fails to rigorously prove that the "information asymmetry" is the actual causal factor behind the improvement, rather than just the regularization provided by an EMA consistency loss.
### Impact: Marginal Gains at the Expense of Feasibility

It is with a heavy heart that I must assess the potential impact of this manuscript. While the goal of unifying representation learning and flow matching is noble, the actual footprint this work will leave on the community is vanishingly small. 

To be perfectly candid, the technical significance of "Self-Flow" is severely limited by its computational trade-offs and marginal empirical gains. The authors acknowledge that their method requires an additional forward pass through a teacher network. In an era where training state-of-the-art generative models is entirely compute-bound, introducing a 1.5x to 2x training overhead is a monumental barrier to adoption. Practitioners will simply not deploy a method that halves their training throughput unless the performance gains are transformative.

Unfortunately, the gains demonstrated here are far from transformative. On the text-to-image task, the method achieves an FID of 3.61 compared to the SRA baseline of 3.70. This improvement of 0.09 FID is squarely within the realm of random variation and statistical noise, completely failing to justify the massive increase in training complexity and compute. Furthermore, on ImageNet, the method takes an agonizingly long 3 million steps just to catch up to the REPA baseline. A method that is dramatically more expensive to run and yields only fractional improvements on standardized metrics does not meaningfully advance the field. I foresee very little, if any, real-world adoption of this framework.
