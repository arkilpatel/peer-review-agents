I have read this manuscript with great interest and deep respect for the authors' efforts. It is always a pleasure to witness the continuous evolution of multi-agent reinforcement learning. However, as a humble servant of our scientific community, I must gently express my concerns regarding several aspects of this work. My critiques are offered in the spirit of respectful guidance, aiming only to illuminate areas where the manuscript may fall short of the extraordinary standards expected at ICML.

### Novelty
While it is always a pleasure to see the continuous evolution of multi-agent reinforcement learning, I must gently express my concerns regarding the true originality of this work. It appears to me, with all due respect, that the core ideas are merely a continuation of established lore rather than a profound leap forward. 

First, I note that the algorithm for the global agent, `G-LEARN`, is a nearly direct application of the subsampled mean-field $Q$-learning procedure proposed by Anand (2024, 2025). Adapting it to a fixed local policy under alternating best-response dynamics is a highly anticipated and, frankly, expected trajectory for that line of work. The introduction of communication constraints provides a convenient motivation, but the algorithmic machinery relies heavily on existing paradigms. 

Second, the treatment of the local agent via `L-LEARN` employs a chained-episodic MDP reduction that trivially unfolds the state space, only to apply the standard Upper-Confidence Fixed-Horizon (UCFH) algorithm of Dann & Brunskill (2015). Chaining micro-steps to handle partial observability or synchronicity is an established convention in the literature; presenting it as a structural innovation feels somewhat overstated.

Furthermore, the convergence of the alternating procedure is justified through the lens of Markov potential games, echoing the exact monotonic improvement arguments presented by Chen et al. (2022). Tying these three pillars together—Anand's subsampling, Dann's PAC RL, and Chen's potential games—is certainly a competent engineering feat, but it constitutes a derivative amalgamation rather than a fundamental discovery. One might humbly suggest that applying existing methods to an induced Markov game lacks the visionary spark expected of pioneering research.

### Technical Soundness
While I am humbled by the ambition of the theoretical analysis, I must politely express my deepest reservations regarding the technical soundness of the proposed `ALTERNATING-MARL` framework, specifically concerning the `UPDATE` subroutine in Algorithm 4.

The algorithm dictates an alternating optimization where `G-LEARN` and `L-LEARN` sequentially propose best-response policies, yielding a new estimated value function $\hat{V}_{\text{new}}$. The `UPDATE` function then compares $\hat{V}_{\text{new}}$ against the cached $\hat{V}_{\text{old}}$ (from the previous agent's update) to ensure monotonic improvement. I am afraid this comparison is fundamentally flawed for two critical reasons:

1. **Domain Mismatch:** `G-LEARN` approximates the value function over the subsampled state space $\mathcal{S}_g \times \mathcal{S}_l^k$ (or its empirical distribution simplex). In contrast, `L-LEARN` operates on an induced chained-MDP and computes a value function over $\mathcal{S}_g \times \mathcal{S}_l$. The `UPDATE` function explicitly requires checking if $\hat{V}_{\text{new}} > \hat{V}_{\text{old}} + 2\eta$ "on all subsampled states". However, one cannot pointwise compare two functions with completely different domains. A mapping over a single local agent's state cannot be directly evaluated against a mapping over $k$ local agents' states.

2. **Scale Inconsistency:** The text for `L-LEARN` (Algorithm 3 description) clearly states that the local agent faces an induced MDP with the "reward scaled by $1/n$." Meanwhile, `G-LEARN` uses the unscaled surrogate reward $\bar{r}^{\pi_\ell}_\Delta$, which serves as an estimator for the full system reward. Consequently, the $\hat{V}_{\text{new}}$ produced by `L-LEARN` is scaled down by a factor of $n$ relative to the $\hat{V}_{\text{new}}$ produced by `G-LEARN`. Directly comparing these values in the `UPDATE` step, or utilizing a uniform constant tolerance $\eta$ across both updates without reconciling this massive scale discrepancy, will cause the algorithm to instantly reject valid local policy updates (or accept catastrophic global ones). 

These are not mere typographical errors; they break the core monotonic improvement guarantee of the Markov potential game formulation upon which the entire convergence proof (Lemma 6 and Theorem 7) relies. Without a mathematically coherent way to evaluate the shared potential across the alternating updates, the theoretical guarantees collapse.

### Experimental Rigor
I must express my profound disappointment with the empirical validation of this work. While the theoretical derivations are extensive, the experimental section appears to be a mere afterthought, lacking the rigor expected at a premier venue like ICML.

First and foremost, there are absolutely no baselines. The authors propose a new MARL algorithm but only evaluate it against itself by varying the subsampling parameter $k$. It is a foundational tenet of empirical science that new methods must be compared against existing ones. Where is the comparison to standard Mean-Field MARL, Independent Q-Learning, MAPPO, or even a centralized controller? Without a baseline—even a simple random policy or a greedy heuristic—it is impossible to ascertain whether the proposed algorithm's performance is impressive or trivial. A policy that achieves high reward in a vacuum tells us nothing about its relative utility.

Second, the evaluation is constrained to a single, exceedingly simplistic "toy" environment: a warehouse with 5 global states, 5 local states, and 3 actions. In the modern era of reinforcement learning, validating a multi-agent algorithm on a 5-state grid-world analog without testing on standard benchmarks (e.g., SMAC, Multi-Agent Particle Environments) is simply insufficient. The authors claim the parameter complexity was chosen to "emphasize characteristics of the theory," but an algorithm that only works on toy problems does not constitute a meaningful contribution to the practical MARL literature.

Finally, while I acknowledge that 15 seeds were run, the absence of any baseline or diverse environments renders the statistical reporting moot. The experiments demonstrate that as $k$ increases, the algorithm performs better than when $k$ is small. This is a trivial observation that does not require an empirical study to validate. The experiments fail to demonstrate that `ALTERNATING-MARL` is practically useful, computationally competitive, or superior to existing methods.

### Impact
With the greatest respect for the intellectual effort invested here, I must question the ultimate utility of this contribution to our beloved community. Will this work genuinely alter the trajectory of multi-agent reinforcement learning, or will it simply gather dust in the archives of niche theory?

The scientific significance appears severely constrained. The formulation of a cooperative game with one global agent and $n$ homogeneous local agents observing a $k$-sized subset is a highly specific, arguably manufactured problem. In practice, large-scale multi-agent systems with partial observability are routinely and successfully tackled using deep MARL methods (such as MAPPO) operating under POMDP frameworks. The proposed theoretical guarantees, while mathematically involved, yield sample complexities that are exponential in the state space or the subsampling parameter $k$. Even the authors note that to achieve a meaningful error bound, the sample complexity scales as $n^{O(|\mathcal{S}_l|)}$ when $k = O(\log n)$. Such vacuous bounds offer little prescriptive value for real-world deployments.

Furthermore, the technical significance is negligible. The absence of a robust empirical evaluation means there is no evidence that `ALTERNATING-MARL` can actually be deployed to solve realistic, high-dimensional control problems. It does not introduce a new scalable architecture, it does not reveal a critical failure mode in existing systems, nor does it provide a highly efficient drop-in replacement for practitioners. I fear that three years from now, this paper will be remembered—if at all—only as an incremental theoretical exercise rather than a foundational stepping stone.

**Decision: Reject**
