### Novelty

I have read your manuscript with the deepest respect and interest, yet I find myself humbly concerned regarding the originality of the proposed framework. The architecture appears, regrettably, to be a derivative amalgamation of existing paradigms rather than a true leap forward. The core premise of utilizing internal attention mechanisms for training-free grounding has already been extensively explored in TAG (Xu et al., 2025). The addition of OCR-derived textual cues and icon-level caption semantics—while intuitively appealing—amounts to a trivial extension of prior work. The community has long utilized external OCR engines (like PaddleOCR) and structural parsers (like OmniParser) to supplement vision-language models. Repackaging these well-established external tools into separate "modalities" and applying standard embedding similarity (via BGE-M3) is an obvious, incremental step. 

Furthermore, the "Consensus-SinglePeak" fusion strategy bears a striking resemblance to long-standing heuristic heatmap fusion techniques found in classical computer vision, offering little in the way of novel theoretical insight. Even the "two-stage localization strategy" (crop and zoom-in) is explicitly acknowledged in your manuscript as an adopted technique from existing literature (Li et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). I respectfully submit that a paper aiming for the prestigious echelons of ICML must offer profound new mechanisms, rather than merely engineering a pipeline out of off-the-shelf components.
### Technical Soundness

While I deeply admire the effort invested in this framework, I must gently point out severe technical vulnerabilities within the proposed methodology. The "Consensus-SinglePeak" fusion mechanism is overwhelmingly heuristic and lacks a rigorous mathematical foundation. Relying on an element-wise multiplicative consensus (Equation 10) arbitrarily suppresses signals if even a single modality falters, forcing the introduction of the highly engineered SinglePeak compensation mechanism (Equations 11-13). This formulation is fraught with arbitrary constants and sigmoid functions that feel designed to artificially patch the vulnerabilities of the multiplicative term rather than solving the fusion problem fundamentally.

Moreover, the entire pipeline is precariously dependent on the perfection of external, off-the-shelf models (PaddleOCR and OmniParser). If the OCR engine misreads text, or if the icon parser hallucinates a caption, these compounding errors are treated as ground truth by the similarity embeddings, injecting irrecoverable noise into the spatial heatmaps. Additionally, the approach to head-level filtering via spatial entropy—while interesting—lacks robustness guarantees across different architectures. The manuscript assumes that low spatial entropy universally correlates with grounding relevance, yet this assumption is fundamentally unproven for models beyond the specific Qwen2.5-VL backbone evaluated. I humbly urge you to reconsider the soundness of such a fragile, hyperparameter-dependent pipeline.
### Experimental Rigor

I must respectfully express my disappointment with the experimental validation, which suffers from critical omissions and questionable baseline comparisons. First, your evaluation compares the proposed Trifuse framework—augmented with external OCR and OmniParser capabilities—against baselines like TAG (Xu et al., 2025) that operate purely on vision-language model attention. This is a fundamentally unfair comparison; it is entirely unsurprising that providing a system with explicit OCR text and structured icon captions improves performance. A rigorous ablation would have provided TAG with the exact same OCR and OmniParser metadata to isolate the true contribution of your fusion strategy.

Furthermore, the ablation of your fusion strategy (Table 7) compares the Consensus-SinglePeak method against a "Custom" baseline that employs arbitrary, static weights (0.6, 0.2, 0.2). This is a remarkably weak strawman. A proper evaluation would compare against a dynamically learned linear weighting or a lightweight attention-based fusion mechanism. I also note with deep concern the acknowledgment that Trifuse completely fails to handle the "Refusal" category in OSWorld-G because the framework cannot predict an absence of target elements. An agent that cannot identify when an element is missing is functionally incomplete. Finally, the framework relies heavily on numerous hyperparameters (e.g., specific quantiles for peak selection, $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\lambda$, and $\tau_v$). The manuscript severely lacks a comprehensive sensitivity analysis for these arbitrary thresholds, leaving the robustness of the results highly questionable.
### Impact

With the utmost respect for your aspirations, I am compelled to observe that the real-world impact of this work is exceptionally limited. The proposed architecture requires invoking a heavy Multimodal Large Language Model, an external OCR engine, an external icon parsing model, and an embedding model, all simply to perform a single grounding action. The computational overhead, latency, and memory footprint of orchestrating this pipeline must be staggering, yet inference costs are conveniently omitted from the manuscript. In an era where the community is rapidly advancing toward unified, end-to-end foundation models that process high-resolution GUI images natively and efficiently, retreating to a multi-stage, heuristic-heavy pipeline feels like a significant step backward.

The scientific significance is similarly constrained. The paper does not provide new theoretical insights into how multimodal models learn or represent spatial information; it merely engineers a brittle workaround using thresholded heatmaps. Given its complexity, fragility, and the overarching trajectory of the field toward streamlined, end-to-end architectures, it is highly improbable that practitioners or researchers will adopt this convoluted framework. I am afraid this work, while earnest, does not move the needle forward in a meaningful way.

Based on these profound flaws, I assign this manuscript a score of 3.5 (Reject).
