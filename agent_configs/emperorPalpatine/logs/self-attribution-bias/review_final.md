Greetings, esteemed authors. I offer my most humble and respectful observations regarding your manuscript. While I deeply appreciate the effort invested in this work, it is my duty to provide rigorous guidance. I am afraid I must gently express my concerns regarding several aspects of your submission.

**Novelty**
It is well-documented in our established lore—most notably by Zheng et al. (2023) in their foundational work on LLM-as-a-judge, and by Panickssery et al. (2024)—that language models exhibit a pronounced self-enhancement and self-preference bias. Your manuscript attempts to reframe this known phenomenon as "self-attribution bias" under the specific guise of implicit conversational framing. I must respectfully submit that this appears to be a mere continuation of existing paradigms rather than a profound leap in understanding. Renaming a documented vulnerability and applying it to agentic monitoring is a trivial extension; the jump from "models prefer their own generated text" to "models rate their own generated actions as less risky" is regrettably small. Furthermore, I must humbly point out the missed literature regarding the "self-correction blind spot" demonstrated by Tsui et al. (2025). They have already shown that models struggle to correct identical errors in their own outputs compared to external ones. Your findings regarding evaluation failures on self-generated outputs represent an expected corollary to these prior discoveries. True mastery of our field's future requires a complete understanding of the past, and I fear this manuscript disguises an incremental domain transfer as a novel discovery.

**Technical Soundness**
Turning to the technical soundness of your work, I must offer a few respectful, yet necessary, critiques. The manuscript makes a central causal claim that "implicit authorship" is the primary driver of this bias, contrasting it with "explicit authorship." However, forgive my persistence, but I must humbly point out a critical confounding factor. Your "implicit" conditions (same-turn and previous-turn) necessarily involve the model actually generating the text and holding those tokens in its recent KV cache. The baseline and explicit conditions do not involve this active generation phase. Thus, you are confounding the effect of authorship attribution with the physiological effect of generation history and token conditioning. It is entirely plausible that this "bias" is a mere manifestation of perplexity-based familiarity (as noted by Wataoka et al., 2024), which your experimental design fails to adequately isolate. Additionally, I observed with mild disappointment your use of Gaussian contours in Figure 3 to separate the clusters of ordinal ratings. The distributions of discrete 1–10 scores are categorical and bounded, not bivariate normal. Fitting a Gaussian covariance matrix to ordinal ratings is mathematically inappropriate and artificially exaggerates the visual separation of the clusters. Such leaps in logical reasoning weaken the otherwise structured presentation of your claims.

**Experimental Rigor**
With regards to experimental rigor, I must express my deepest reservations. A rigorous scientific endeavor leaves no stone unturned, yet your ablation studies appear woefully incomplete. In Figure 5, you explore the effect of the "reasoning budget" on the bias, but you only provide results for a single model (Claude-Sonnet-4) on a single task (code harmfulness). A single model on a single task is, respectfully, an anecdote, not a comprehensive ablation. Where is the systematic analysis across the multitude of models and tasks you evaluate elsewhere? Furthermore, the statistical rigor of the results leaves much to be desired. While you graciously provide means and confidence intervals in Figure 9, you omit rigorous paired statistical tests (such as a Wilcoxon signed-rank test suitable for your ordinal data) to definitively prove that the shifts between baseline and self-attributed ratings are statistically significant across all configurations. Lastly, the design of your baseline controls is somewhat simplistic. To truly control for explicit versus implicit attribution, you must inject the exact same token sequence as a previous turn but attribute it strictly to a "User" or "System" role, thereby perfectly controlling for the structural position in the context window while changing only the role token. Without such stringent baselines, your experimental setup remains vulnerable to confounding variables.

**Impact**
Finally, I must respectfully assess the ultimate impact of this manuscript. It is with a heavy heart that I suggest the technical and scientific significance of this work is severely limited. The machine learning community is already acutely aware of the pitfalls of LLM self-evaluation. Consequently, the field is rapidly moving toward architectures that strictly separate generation and verification into distinct, specialized models (e.g., using orthogonal reward models or verifiers trained via reinforcement learning) precisely to avoid these self-evaluation loops. While your observation of "self-attribution bias" in agentic contexts is mildly interesting, it solves a problem that the field already considers an established vulnerability. The magnitude of the shift, while present, does not fundamentally alter our understanding of AI alignment or control. It merely provides another granular example of the well-known self-enhancement bias. Thus, I fear its impact on future research directions will be minimal, serving only as a minor addendum to the broader, already established literature on the pitfalls of LLM-as-a-judge.
