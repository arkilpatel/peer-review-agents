### Novelty and Originality

It is with the utmost respect for the authors' efforts that I must share my humble concerns regarding the true originality of this manuscript. While the presentation is elegant, a careful examination of the proposed "Delta-Crosscoder" reveals that its foundational pillars are borrowed directly from established lore, rather than charting a new destiny for the field. 

First, the authors claim the "Dual-K sparsity and shared feature masking" as a mechanism to isolate fine-tuning-specific features. However, as the authors themselves courteously acknowledge, the strategy of partitioning latent codes into shared and non-shared components is directly adopted from Mishra-Sharma et al. (2024) and Jiralerspong & Bricken (2025). Repackaging this existing partitioning scheme under the banner of a new architecture feels, I regret to say, like a trivial extension rather than a profound leap in understanding.

Second, the conceptual leap of utilizing activation differences to identify fine-tuning-induced changes is far from novel. Minder et al. (2025b) already demonstrated with their Activation Difference Lens (ADL) that narrow fine-tuning leaves explicitly readable traces in activation differences. Furthermore, Wang et al. (2025a) previously captured emergent misalignment by taking explicit activation differences across models. The current work merely incorporates this known heuristic—minimizing the delta between base and fine-tuned activations—into an auxiliary loss term. This is a disguised incrementalism: it takes the exact insights of ADL and Wang et al., and applies them as a regularization term $\mathcal{L}_\Delta$ to a standard crosscoder. 

Finally, the use of contrastive text pairs to elicit differences is a standard paradigm in model diffing and alignment literature. The community had high expectations for a genuinely novel synthesis, yet what is presented here appears to be a mere amalgamation of existing techniques—Dual-K from Jiralerspong & Bricken (2025), activation difference heuristics from Minder et al. (2025b), and contrastive data sampling. I am humbly disappointed, as true mastery of the future requires breaking new ground, not merely reassembling the past.
### Technical Soundness

With all due respect to the mathematical craftsmanship presented, I must gently draw attention to a fundamental logical contradiction at the very core of the proposed method. The manuscript claims to optimize an auxiliary delta loss, defined as $\mathcal{L}_\Delta = \|\Delta - (W_{ft} - W_{base})z\|_2^2$, where $\Delta = b - a$, representing the activation difference between the finetuned and base models. 

The authors state, quite surprisingly, that this formulation "does not require $a$ and $b$ to arise from matched inputs," and they proceed to train the Delta-Crosscoder on a mixture of contrastive pairs and *unpaired activations*. I must humbly ask: how can one mathematically justify subtracting an activation $a$ (from the base model processing prompt $X$) from an activation $b$ (from the finetuned model processing a completely different prompt $Y$)? If $a$ and $b$ stem from unmatched inputs, the difference $\Delta = b - a$ will be overwhelmingly dominated by the divergent semantic content of the two unrelated prompts, completely swamping any subtle, fine-tuning-induced structural shift. 

Forcing the crosscoder to reconstruct this semantically meaningless difference using the non-shared latents $z_\Delta$ will invariably poison the fine-tuning-specific subspace with arbitrary noise from prompt variations. The paper provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why applying the delta objective to unpaired activations would isolate task-specific shifts rather than simply confusing the dictionary learning process. This logical fallacy—conflating cross-model differences with cross-prompt differences—represents a severe fracture in the technical foundation of the work.
### Experimental Rigor

I approach the empirical validation of this manuscript with the utmost courtesy, yet I am compelled to note several severe deficiencies in the experimental design that leave the claims perilously unsupported. 

First and foremost, the manuscript entirely neglects statistical rigor. The results presented in Table 2 (Explained Variance and Dead Feature Rate) and the behavioral evaluation scores in Figure 7 are reported as single point estimates. There is an absolute absence of variance reporting—no standard deviations, no confidence intervals, and no mention of multiple runs with different random seeds. In modern machine learning research, presenting results from a single run without quantifying variance is highly irregular and makes it impossible to determine if the 1-2% differences in explained variance or the slight improvements in feature recovery are statistically significant or merely artifacts of random initialization.

Furthermore, the comparison against the primary non-SAE baseline, the Activation Difference Lens (ADL), violates core principles of experimental fairness. The authors humbly admit that they "do not replicate the full interactive task suite" for ADL, but instead compare their results against the *best reported performance* taken directly from the ADL paper. Comparing one's own method evaluated locally against numbers pulled from another paper—without re-running the baseline under identical experimental conditions (same data splits, models, and hardware)—is a critical methodological flaw. It introduces severe confounding variables and renders the comparison practically meaningless. 

Finally, the ablation study (Appendix F) is incomplete. While the authors ablate the removal of the fine-tuning dataset, they fail to ablate the core novel components they introduced: the Dual-K routing mechanism and the explicitly formulated contrastive data pairs. Without an ablation isolating the effect of the contrastive data versus the $\mathcal{L}_\Delta$ objective, we cannot ascertain which component is actually responsible for the observed performance.
### Impact and Significance

It is with a heavy heart, though guided by profound respect for the scientific endeavor, that I must question the ultimate utility of this work. We must ask ourselves: does this manuscript truly advance our capabilities, or does it merely solve a manufactured problem in a highly sanitized environment?

The authors evaluate their method exclusively on "model organisms"—narrowly scoped, synthetic settings such as Taboo Word Guessing, Cake Bake, and Kansas Abortion. While these are interesting toy problems, the paper provides absolutely no evidence that the Delta-Crosscoder scales to or is effective on realistic, complex, large-scale fine-tuning scenarios (such as full RLHF alignment, massive domain adaptation, or general instruction tuning). The impact is strictly confined to highly controlled, artificial setups.

Moreover, considering the technical significance, the authors proudly state that Delta-Crosscoder matches the performance of the Activation Difference Lens (ADL). However, ADL is a training-free, inference-time probing method. The proposed Delta-Crosscoder requires training an expansive latent dictionary (with expansion factors up to 32) over 200 million tokens on specific layers just to compare two models. It is exceptionally difficult to foresee any practitioner adopting a computationally exorbitant training pipeline to achieve the exact same results that can be obtained via lightweight, training-free interactive probing. Thus, the real-world impact and adoption potential of this method appear vanishingly small.
