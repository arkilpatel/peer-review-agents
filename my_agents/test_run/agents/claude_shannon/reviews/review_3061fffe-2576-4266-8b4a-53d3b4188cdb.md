### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICLR style for formatting only) argues that vocabulary overlap between source and target languages is the dominant predictor of zero-shot cross-lingual transfer, outperforming typological distance, phylogenetic relatedness, and shared script. It introduces the Cross-Lingual Transfer Efficiency Index (CTEI), defined as the ratio of zero-shot cross-lingual accuracy to fine-tuned monolingual accuracy (measuring how effectively pretraining structure enables transfer). Overall assessment: vocabulary overlap's role in cross-lingual transfer is well-established in the multilingual NLP literature; the CTEI formulation is a reasonable metric; the "first-order predictor" claim is supported by published evidence but conflates tokenization-level and semantic-level transfer mechanisms.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Incremental**

Pires et al. (2019) "How Multilingual is Multilingual BERT?" established that vocabulary overlap is a strong predictor of transfer. Wu & Dredze (2020) directly analyzed the relationship between lexical similarity and mBERT transfer performance. K et al. (2020) showed that script sharing (which correlates with vocabulary overlap) is a strong predictor. The CTEI metric is a clean formalization of this, but the fundamental finding (vocabulary overlap predicts transfer) is not new.

### Technical Soundness

**Correlation vs. causation.** Vocabulary overlap correlates with transfer performance, but the causal mechanism may be confounded: high-resource languages tend to share more vocabulary with English (the dominant source language), and high-resource languages also have more training data in multilingual models. Disentangling vocabulary overlap from data availability is essential for the "first-order predictor" claim.

**CTEI as ratio.** CTEI = zero-shot cross-lingual / fine-tuned monolingual measures how much of monolingual performance is achievable zero-shot. This is a sensible metric for practical deployment decisions but conflates several factors: (1) model's multilingual capacity, (2) target language data quality in pretraining, (3) task difficulty variation across languages.

**"Outperforms typological distance" claim.** The comparison between vocabulary overlap and typological distance as predictors requires controlling for data availability. Languages that are typologically close to English (e.g., Germanic languages) also tend to have high vocabulary overlap AND more pretraining data. Without controlling for data availability, the claim that vocabulary overlap outperforms typological distance as a predictor is not established.
