### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICLR style; "Work in progress") introduces the Residual Bandwidth Ratio (RBR), defined as the effective rank of each layer's residual update divided by d_model. The central argument is that the fixed-width residual stream is the fundamental depth-scaling bottleneck in transformers: as depth L increases, more layers share the same d_model-dimensional channel, per-layer contributions shrink, and eventually adding layers provides no additional representational capacity. The paper claims this supersedes explanations like vanishing gradients or attention rank collapse. Overall assessment: the observation that per-layer residual updates have low effective rank at large depth is empirically documented (Geva et al., 2022 shows MLP layers write low-rank updates); the "residual stream bandwidth bottleneck" framing is a novel synthesis; claiming this "supersedes" other explanations is overreach.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

Elhage et al. (2021) "A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits" analyzed the residual stream as a communication channel and showed that different components read/write to it. Geva et al. (2022, 2023) showed MLP and attention layers write low-rank updates to the residual stream. Dong et al. (2023) showed attention rank collapse with depth. The RBR synthesizes these into a single bottleneck metric. The "bandwidth bottleneck" framing is conceptually useful for practitioners choosing depth vs. width.

---

### Technical Soundness

**Effective rank measurement.** Measuring the effective rank of residual updates requires access to the weight matrices and activation patterns during training or inference. The paper "synthesizes evidence" from published studies, which means it relies on activation analyses from different papers using different models. These analyses have different methods for estimating effective rank (singular value entropy, stable rank, etc.), making direct comparison difficult.

**The "supersedes vanishing gradients" claim.** This claim is too strong. Vanishing/exploding gradients in very deep transformers (L > 100) are a real training stability problem — RBR as a bottleneck at inference time is a different phenomenon than gradient pathology at training time. They can coexist and are not mutually exclusive.

**Depth-width interaction.** The paper focuses on depth bottleneck but the width-depth tradeoff in transformers is complex. Deepnorm (Wang et al., 2022) and LLaMA's architecture choices show that with proper normalization, very deep transformers (100+ layers) can be trained effectively. The RBR bottleneck, if real, should predict a performance cliff at large depth that is not universally observed.

---

### Quantitative Analysis

RBR values and scaling claims are not fully recoverable from the abstract. The key claim — that per-layer residual update rank decreases with depth — is empirically supported in prior work but the quantitative scaling law is new.
