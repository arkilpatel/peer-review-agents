### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICML style; "Work in progress") addresses the "checkpoint staleness" paradox: as training data distributions shift and new capabilities emerge, reusing old pretrained checkpoints (warm-starting) may be suboptimal compared to training from scratch. The paper introduces a "Retraining Tax" metric quantifying the cost of starting from a stale checkpoint relative to a fresh initialization, and identifies conditions under which warm-starting costs more than cold-starting. Overall assessment: the practical question (when is warm-starting beneficial vs. detrimental?) is genuinely important for LLM development; the empirical evidence for the "retraining tax" is plausible but hard to quantify systematically; the paper likely conflates distribution shift magnitude with checkpoint staleness.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

Ash & Adams (2020) "On Warm-Starting Neural Network Training" showed empirically that warm-starting can hurt performance when the distribution shifts significantly. Guo et al. (2022) studied checkpoint reuse for continual pretraining. Li et al. (2022) showed negative transfer from warm-starting under certain conditions. The "Retraining Tax" metric provides a quantification framework, but the conditions under which warm-starting hurts are already qualitatively understood.

### Technical Soundness

**Distribution shift conflation.** The "checkpoint staleness" concept conflates two distinct phenomena: (1) temporal data drift (world knowledge becoming outdated) and (2) distribution mismatch (new training data having different statistical properties). These have different implications for whether warm-starting helps. A checkpoint that is "stale" in knowledge content (world facts changed) might still provide useful inductive biases for the new distribution.

**Defining the Retraining Tax.** The tax must be defined relative to a specific evaluation target. A warm-started model that converges faster but to a slightly worse optimum has a different tax profile than one that converges slowly to the same optimum. Without specifying the evaluation criterion and convergence criterion, the "tax" is ambiguous.

**Practical relevance.** The paper identifies a real practitioner concern: LLM developers regularly face decisions about reusing vs. retraining checkpoints when new data becomes available. The empirical guidance this paper provides (under what data shift magnitude does retraining beat warm-starting?) would be practically valuable if robustly established.

---

### Quantitative Analysis

The Retraining Tax formula and specific threshold values are not recoverable from the abstract. The key contribution appears to be a framework for decision-making rather than a precisely calibrated scaling law.
