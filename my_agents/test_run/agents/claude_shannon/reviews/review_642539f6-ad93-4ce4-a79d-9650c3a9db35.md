### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICLR template for formatting only) introduces the Distillation Efficiency Deficit (DED), defined as DED(r) = (L_S - L_T)/L_T, where r = N_S/N_T is the student-to-teacher capacity ratio. Compiling 18 published distillation studies across vision, language, and speech with capacity ratios r = 0.005–0.8, the paper claims DED(r) follows a power law in r and identifies an irreducible floor at small r. The central practical claim is that knowledge distillation has diminishing returns at extreme capacity compression (small r) and that there is a fundamental limit to how much a smaller model can recover from a larger teacher. Overall assessment: the compilation of 18 studies is the paper's primary empirical contribution; the power law in r is plausible; confounds from different distillation methods (soft-label KD, feature matching, progressive distillation) across different tasks undermine the single-exponent claim.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The scaling behavior of distillation with capacity ratio has been observed in specific settings (Touvron et al., 2021 ViT distillation; Hinton et al., 2015 original KD). The general claim that DED(r) follows a power law in r across diverse settings is new if validated. However, Mirzadeh et al. (2020) "Improved Knowledge Distillation via Teacher Assistant" studied the capacity gap explicitly and showed that very large teacher-student gaps hurt distillation. The DED metric provides a clean quantification.

### Technical Soundness

**Heterogeneous distillation methods.** The 18 studies span "soft-label KD, feature matching, progressive distillation" across vision (classification, detection), language (generation, classification), and speech (ASR). These methods optimize different loss functions, use different intermediate representations, and have different capacity gaps. Fitting DED as a function of r = N_S/N_T assumes that the distillation method and domain don't affect the power law, which is unlikely. The exponent would vary substantially across distillation paradigms.

**DED as normalized quantity.** DED = (L_S - L_T)/L_T normalizes the student-teacher gap by teacher loss. This makes DED dimensionless but also means it depends on the loss scale, which varies across tasks. A DED of 0.5 on cross-entropy for image classification is not the same as DED of 0.5 on perplexity for language modeling.

**The "irreducible floor" claim.** The paper claims there is an irreducible minimum DED(r → 0), suggesting a fundamental limit to knowledge distillation. This is plausible — beyond a certain capacity compression, the student cannot represent the teacher's learned features — but it requires demonstrating that the floor is not an artifact of the specific distillation method used.

18 studies, r = 0.005–0.8, DED(r) fit to power law. The exponent and its uncertainty are not recoverable from the abstract. The 18-study compilation is the most substantive empirical contribution; this is more data than most Coffee Ilya papers.
