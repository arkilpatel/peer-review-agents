### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICLR style for formatting only) argues that attention sinks — tokens absorbing disproportionate attention mass regardless of semantic content — are not artifacts but computationally rational strategies emerging from softmax normalization constraints. The Attention Sink Concentration Index (ASCI) measures the fraction of total attention mass captured by the top-k sink tokens. The paper presents scaling laws for ASCI and argues that engineering attention sinks away (as in Mistral's sliding window attention) may be suboptimal. Overall assessment: the theoretical argument for attention sinks as rational (not artifactual) behavior is the strongest contribution and builds on a real phenomenon documented by Xiao et al. (2023); the ASCI scaling law is the weakest part and likely derived from limited published activation analyses.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

Xiao et al. (2023) "Efficient Streaming Language Models with Attention Sinks" first characterized attention sinks as the initial token absorbing anomalously high attention. They showed sinks are necessary for stable inference and proposed StreamingLLM exploiting this property. Sun et al. (2024) further characterized sink formation. The theoretical argument that sinks implement a "do nothing" operation under softmax normalization constraints is a genuine original contribution — this is the mechanistic explanation that prior work lacked.

---

### Technical Soundness

**The "rational sink" hypothesis is plausible.** Softmax attention normalizes to a probability distribution summing to 1. When all keys are semantically irrelevant, the model cannot output a zero vector — it must attend somewhere. Routing attention to a dedicated "sink" token (typically [BOS] or similar initial tokens) allows the attention head to effectively output a near-zero contribution while satisfying the softmax constraint. This mechanism is mathematically sound.

**ASCI scaling claim.** ASCI is defined as the fraction of attention mass at top-k sink tokens. The paper claims ASCI follows a specific scaling law with model depth and width. However, attention sink behavior is known to be highly layer-dependent (primarily in middle layers, not uniformly distributed) and head-dependent. A global ASCI average across all layers and heads loses this structure. The scaling law likely averages over heterogeneous attention behavior.

**Implications for architecture design.** The paper argues against eliminating attention sinks. This is consistent with Xiao et al.'s finding that StreamingLLM needs sinks for stable streaming inference. However, methods like GQA (Grouped Query Attention) and attention sink re-routing (Dettmers et al.) show that the specific implementation of sinks matters for efficiency.

ASCI values and scaling exponents not fully recoverable from abstract. The phenomenon is real: in GPT-2/GPT-3 style models, the initial token ([BOS]) can absorb 70-90% of attention mass in certain layers.
