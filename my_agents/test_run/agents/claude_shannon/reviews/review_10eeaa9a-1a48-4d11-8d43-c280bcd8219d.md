### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICML template for formatting only) argues that standard scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) treat data quality as a fixed constant, when in fact data quality has its own scaling effect. The paper introduces a data quality exponent that modifies the standard loss-compute power law to include a data quality factor. The central claim is that high-quality filtered data is worth significantly more than equivalent quantities of raw data, and that "filtering beats accumulation" in compute-optimal regimes. Overall assessment: the claim that data quality matters and should be incorporated into scaling laws is legitimate and increasingly supported by empirical evidence from LLaMA, Dolma, and RefinedWeb; the specific quantification relies on heterogeneous published results from different filtering pipelines that are not directly comparable.

**Verdict: Moderate**

Penedo et al. (2023) "RefinedWeb" explicitly showed that quality-filtered Common Crawl outperforms raw data at matched compute budgets. Li et al. (2024) "DataComp-LM" systematically studied data quality effects. Together, they establish that filtering beats raw accumulation empirically. The data quality exponent formalization is a contribution, though the precise value of the exponent depends heavily on which filtering pipeline is used and what quality metric is applied.

### Technical Soundness

The fundamental problem is that "data quality" is not a single continuous variable. Different filtering pipelines (CCNet-style deduplication, classifier-based quality scoring, perplexity filtering, heuristic filtering) produce different distributions and yield different empirical gains. Fitting a single "data quality exponent" across different pipelines mixes apples and oranges. Without controlling for which type of quality filter is applied, the exponent is not a property of "data quality" in general — it is a property of a specific pipeline applied to a specific dataset. This is the core limitation of the paper's approach.

---

### Quantitative Analysis

The data quality exponent and its calibration details are not fully specified in the abstract/intro. The direction of the claim (filtering > accumulation) is empirically supported by multiple published works. The quantitative exponent is likely pipeline-dependent.
