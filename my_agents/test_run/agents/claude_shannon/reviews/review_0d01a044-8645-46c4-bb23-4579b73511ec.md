### Summary

This ICLR 2026 paper introduces the Single Index Bandit (SIB) problem, extending generalized linear bandits (GLBs) to the setting where the reward function f(·) is unknown. The key algorithmic contribution is a Stein's method-based estimator for the unknown parameter θ* that achieves optimal minimax rates without knowledge of f(·). Three algorithms are proposed: STOR (explore-then-commit, O(T^{2/3}) regret), ESTOR (epoch-based, Õ(T^{1/2}) near-optimal regret for monotone f), and GSTOR (double-exploration with kernel regression for general f, O(T^{3/4}) regret). The paper also handles sparse high-dimensional settings. This is a strong theoretical contribution filling a genuine gap in the bandit literature, backed by solid proofs and targeted experiments.

---

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Substantial**

The paper identifies a genuine and practically important gap: all existing GLB algorithms assume known link function f(·), and as shown in prior work, minor misspecification leads to linear regret. The SIB formulation (joint learning of θ* and f(·)) is new to the bandit literature. The key technical novelty is applying Stein's identity to derive an estimator that decouples the unknown f(·) from the parameter estimation:

E[y_i S(x_i)] = μ* θ*   (where μ* = E[f'(X^T θ*)])

This observation bypasses the need for an explicit likelihood, enabling a closed-form O(nd) estimator with no iterative optimization — a significant computational advantage. The epoch-based schedule in ESTOR achieving Õ(T^{1/2}) is also non-trivial; the authors correctly note that the EtC-based STOR only achieves T^{2/3} and motivate the epoch approach carefully.

The concurrent work mentioned (batched MAB with SIM approach using dynamic binning) addresses a different setting. The distinction is well-articulated in Appendix A.

One caveat: Stein's method has been applied in bandit contexts before (low-rank matrix bandits, as noted in §1), and the core estimator in Eq. (1) builds on existing SIM estimation literature (particularly score-based estimators). The genuine advance is the online/bandit adaptation, the truncation technique for heavy-tailed rewards, and the tight regret analysis.

### Technical Soundness

The technical content appears solid. Key assessments:

**Theorem 3.1 (SIM estimator):** The bound ||θ̂ − μ*θ*||_2 = Õ(√{d/n}) matches the minimax rate for linear regression (which is a special case). The proof sketch is credible: Stein's identity for E[y_i S(x_i)] = μ*θ* under any differentiable density, truncation via ϕ_τ to handle heavy-tailed rewards, and first-order convexity arguments. The claim that this matches minimax rates is plausible given the connection to linear regression.

**Theorem 3.5/3.6 (ESTOR regret):** The Õ(T^{1/2}) result uses an epoch-based refinement schedule. The key assumption (Assumption 3.3) requires μ_i* = E[f'(X^T θ*)] > 0 across epochs — a monotonicity condition that the authors carefully relate back to the GLM canonical form. The regret decomposition splits into exploration cost and exploitation gap; the epoch lengths (e_i via geometric schedule) are designed to balance these.

**Theorem 3.9 (GSTOR):** The O(T^{3/4}) result for general f requires Gaussian design and kernel regression. The paper correctly notes that T^{1/2} is likely unachievable in this setting (analogizing to Lipschitz bandit lower bounds), making the T^{3/4} rate the natural target. The condition d^{15} = O(T^2) is unusual and potentially restrictive for large d — this deserves more discussion.

**Potential concern:** The paper claims the GLM canonical form induces monotonically increasing f(·) (§3.2), which would mean SIB strictly generalizes GLB in the monotone case. This claim should be verified — canonical GLMs (logistic, Poisson) have monotone f, but not all GLMs do. The paper's claim appears correct for canonical-link GLMs.

**Citation issue:** As with other papers in this collection, citations render as "?" in the PDF. This prevents reference verification.

### Baseline Fairness Audit

The experimental comparison is reasonable but limited in scope:

**Monotone setting (Figure 1):** LinUCB and LinTS for the linear case, UCB-GLM and GLM-TSL for GLB cases. These are appropriate and represent standard baselines. The misspecified baselines (dashed lines) are a nice addition showing the fragility of existing methods.

**Fairness concern:** The comparison sets up a scenario where GLB baselines are guaranteed to fail (unknown f(·)), then shows ESTOR doesn't fail. This is a valid demonstration of robustness but doesn't characterize the *cost* of agnosticism — how much does ESTOR sacrifice versus a correctly specified GLB baseline (where f is actually known)? From Figure 1, ESTOR appears to match (not lag) the correctly specified GLB methods, which is the paper's strongest experimental finding.

**Missing: real-world comparison with non-adversarial conditions.** The real-world experiments (Forest Cover Type, Yahoo news) in Appendix L compare against GLB baselines where the true link function is unknown, again advantaging ESTOR. A cleaner comparison would hold out a dataset where the true reward function is approximately known and compare directly.

**Sample size:** 20 repetitions is relatively few for bandit experiments; T=10,000 is moderate. Standard deviations are shown in Figure 1, which is appropriate.

### Quantitative Analysis

From Figure 1 (cumulative regret, T=10,000):
- **Linear setting:** ESTOR matches LinUCB/LinTS closely. This confirms there is no significant cost for not knowing f in the linear case.
- **Poisson (f=exp):** ESTOR and STOR outperform GLM methods that are misspecified, and match correctly specified GLB. Correctly specified UCB-GLM appears to have slightly lower regret.
- **Square/Fifth power:** Misspecified GLB baselines (dashed) diverge dramatically; ESTOR remains efficient.

Computational efficiency (Table 1, Appendix L): ESTOR is hundreds of times faster than UCB-GLM and thousands of times faster than GLM-TSL. The O(nd) vs MLE comparison is compelling.

**Regret rate summary:**
- STOR: Õ(T^{2/3}) for monotone f
- ESTOR: Õ(T^{1/2}) for monotone f — matches linear bandit lower bound
- GSTOR: O(T^{3/4}) for general f

The T^{1/2} rate for ESTOR matching the linear bandit minimax lower bound is a strong theoretical result, though it's worth noting the hidden polynomial factors in d, K (number of arms), and problem constants (M, L, σ) could be substantial.

### Qualitative Analysis

The experiments cleanly illustrate the core claim: ESTOR is robust to unknown f(·) while maintaining efficiency comparable to correctly specified baselines. The misspecified GLB curves (dashed) provide a visually compelling demonstration of why the problem matters.

The real-world results (Appendix L) show consistent outperformance over GLB methods, which is expected since in real data the true link function is indeed unknown. However, one wonders whether ESTOR's advantage comes from agnosticism per se or from the robustness of the linear approximation implicit in Stein's estimator.

The LLM embedding visualization analogy from the bandit context is not applicable here — instead, the cluster structure of the bandit problem manifests through the epoch-based exploration schedule.

**Failure mode not addressed:** What happens when μ* = E[f'(X^T θ*)] ≈ 0? The paper assumes μ* ≠ 0 (and μ_i* > 0 for ESTOR). When μ* is small, the estimator becomes ill-conditioned. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but doesn't characterize how small μ* can be before the bounds become vacuous.

---

### Results Explanation

The paper does a good job explaining *why* ESTOR achieves T^{1/2}: the epoch-based schedule allows the exploration phase to shrink as the estimate improves, achieving a favorable regret decomposition. The proof sketch for Theorem 3.1 (applying Stein's identity, truncation, Bernstein's inequality) is illuminating.

Less explained: why GSTOR achieves exactly T^{3/4} rather than some other rate, and whether this is tight. The analogy to Lipschitz bandits is suggestive but not a formal lower bound argument.

### Per-Area Findings

#### Area 1: Stein's Method Estimator and Theoretical Contributions (weight: 0.65)

The Stein's identity-based estimator (Eq. 1) is the technical core of this work. It achieves the minimax rate Õ(√{d/n}) without knowledge of f, in O(nd) time — both are genuine improvements over prior SIM work. The regret analysis for ESTOR (Õ(T^{1/2})) leverages this estimator cleanly. The bound for GSTOR (O(T^{3/4})) under Gaussian design is the expected price for full agnosticism.

**Gap:** Lower bounds for SIBs are not established. Is T^{1/2} tight for monotone f? Is T^{3/4} tight for general f? The paper suggests T^{1/2} is unachievable for general f (by analogy to Lipschitz bandits), but the exact lower bound for SIBs remains open.

#### Area 2: Empirical Validation (weight: 0.35)

The experiments are targeted and well-chosen. The misspecification experiments (Figure 1, cases 3-4) provide the most compelling evidence for practical relevance. The computational advantage (hundreds to thousands of times faster) is strongly demonstrated.

**Gap:** The experiments focus on confirming theoretical predictions rather than stress-testing edge cases. The regime μ* ≈ 0 is not tested, nor are highly non-Gaussian arm distributions.

---

### Synthesis

**Cross-cutting theme:** The paper consistently leverages the simplicity of Stein's identity to bypass the need for explicit knowledge of f(·). This unifying principle makes the contribution cohesive.

**Tension:** The Gaussian design assumption for GSTOR is acknowledged as a limitation but is treated as routine. In bandit applications, Gaussian contexts may be unrealistic. The paper defers "extending these results to more general settings" as future work, but this is arguably a core limitation for practitioners.

**Key open question:** What is the minimax lower bound for SIBs under monotone f? If T^{1/2} is tight (which the paper suggests by matching linear bandit rates), this would be a complete result. Without a matching lower bound, the near-optimality claim for ESTOR is not fully established.

---

### Open Questions

1. **Lower bounds:** What is the minimax regret lower bound for SIBs (monotone and general f)? The T^{1/2} claim for ESTOR is "near-optimal" only relative to linear bandits; a dedicated SIB lower bound is needed.

2. **μ* = 0 robustness:** How does ESTOR degrade when μ* is small? The condition μ_i* > 0 across all epochs (Assumption 3.3) is critical — can this be relaxed?

3. **Non-Gaussian arms for GSTOR:** The T^{3/4} result requires Gaussian design. Is this fundamental, or can kernel regression be generalized to broader distributions?

4. **Comparison with correctly specified GLBs:** The experiments show ESTOR is competitive, but how large is the gap versus a correctly specified GLB when f IS known? This would quantify the price of agnosticism.
