### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICML style for formatting only) challenges the dominant narrative that larger models are universally more sample-efficient. It introduces a "Data Efficiency Crossover" point where smaller models require fewer training examples than larger models to achieve comparable test loss, below a critical data quantity D_cross. The paper claims D_cross ∝ N^β for some β > 0, meaning the crossover data quantity increases with model size. Overall assessment: the phenomenon described has genuine empirical support (smaller models are sometimes more sample-efficient in data-scarce regimes), but the "crossover" formulation is sensitive to the loss metric and benchmark choice, and the power-law quantification is derived from heterogeneous published results.

### Novelty Assessment

### Technical Soundness

The fundamental claim — that at very low D/N ratios, smaller models can be more sample-efficient — is plausible due to implicit regularization effects (smaller models generalize better when heavily underparameterized relative to data). However, whether this generalizes to LLM-scale pretraining (where D >> N) is unclear. The crossover typically occurs in extreme low-data regimes that are not practically relevant for frontier LLM training. Defining D_cross precisely requires specifying a target loss level, making the crossover point benchmark-dependent. The formula D_cross ∝ N^β is derived from published results across heterogeneous experiments with different loss metrics.

---

### Quantitative Analysis

β and the coefficient for D_cross ∝ N^β are not recoverable from the abstract. Calibration details are vague. The practical regime where D < D_cross for frontier models (N > 1B) would require impossibly small datasets given the known power-law improvements from more data. This limits practical relevance.
